An undaunted devotion to the cause of woman suffrage links the name of Alice Stone Blackwell, cofounder of the Boston League of Women Voters, with that of her famous mother, Lucy Stone, a pioneer of woman's rights. Her father, Henry R. Blackwell, devoted his life to obtaining equal rights for women. One of Miss Blackwell's aunts, Elizabeth Blackwell, and her sister, Antoinette Brown-don Blackwell, was the first woman to be ordained a minister. Miss Blackwell herself graduated from Oberlin College in 1853 and soon joined her parents in working for suffrage and in publishing the Woman's Journal, carrying on the work that her parents had started until 1917. It became the Woman Centennial. For many years she appeared annually before the congresses of the International Woman Suffrage Association, and a distinguished lawyer once said he considered her the most effective woman suffrage speaker he had ever seen and ever heard. It was also Miss Blackwell who brought together the two parties of the suffrage movement, welding in 1890 the National Association of women-suffragists and the American Woman Suffrage Association into a powerful fighting organization. For years she has been a trustee of Boston University and is a member of Phi Beta Kappa. Close to the heart is her love of the classics. She has produced in English a volume of Armenian poems, as well as one of Russian poems a volume of Tibetan songs and an English translation of "Medea" and "Phèdre" such a competent American poetry. Her biography of Catherine Breshkovsky, "The Little Grandmother of the Russian Revolution," was some time one of the "six best sellers."
