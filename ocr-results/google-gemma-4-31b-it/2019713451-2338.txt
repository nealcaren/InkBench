SUGGESTIONS. 1. The only possible way of teaching Penmanship successfully in school, is to have all the scholars of the class write the same line in the book at the same time.
2. The order of instruction is KNOWLEDGE, EXECUTION, CRITICISM.
First, KNOWLEDGE. Explain the copy carefully, and have it analyzed into its Principles, and then into its Elements. Teach the nature, peculiarity, sloping, bending and ending of each element; notice the combination of elements, and the way of combining lines between the letters; call attention to similitude and parallelism of parts and lines.
Second, EXECUTION. Let them first trace the copy with dry pens. Then have one in the column written, directing the pupils to be very careful as to position, penhold, rests, and movements, which are explained in the Manual and on cover of No. 1. Do

not allow the pen to be raised from the paper until the line is written except in p. Be sure the wrist does not touch; it should be high enough from the desk to allow a holder to be passed under its right side.
Third, CRITICISM. Criticize the line written, by asking questions on each particular given as knowledge, which the class answer by raising their hands. The errors lie, of course, on each side of the truth. Thus, if the line is a sure, it may be either the wrong way or too much or too little; the slope too much or too little; the angle may be correct, or too broad, or too narrow. Select one of your own observation, for correct direction, faults discovered, by the answers and your own observation, for correct direction, special attention to them, and have the pupils correct their written line that they may avoid the error in the next. Thus, criticize the execution of each line, and direct attention to the correction of each fault until the whole is perfected.

GENERAL RULES. 1. Read the answers. 2. Every principle touches both the head base and lines. 3. The Main Lines are the straight lines and the sides of the oval quadrants downwards; the ovals, the second opposite of 5 and 8, and the fourth and fourth of 2, too Main Lines. 4. The Connecting Lines are the slanted lines and the curves written upwards. The Turns unite main to connecting lines. Sometimes the main and connecting lines unite in a point: this is a termed the Connection. The line formed between two letters by their union is called the Running Line, extending from the upstroke, for the oven, of the turn. 5. There are five Elements, numbered in the order in which they occur in the principles. There are six Principles in the small letters; three from the first, second and third elements; and three from all the small letters together.
