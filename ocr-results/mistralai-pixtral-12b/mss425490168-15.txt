PRESS RELEASE for P.M.s, Thursday, March 30,

a test case on the District of Columbia anti-discrimination laws of 1872 and 1963 will be tried in D.C. Court on Friday, March 31. The case, "Dist. of Columbia v. John R. Thompson Co.," will take place on the 2nd floor, Municipal Court, Criminal Division, 5th St. between E and F Sts., NW, at 11:30 AM.

The Thompson Cafeteria at 725 14th St., NW is to be prosecuted by the DC government counsel on violation of the 1872 and 1963 laws against discrimination because of color in restaurants and other public places. Clark King will be pre-siding attorney. The complaining witnesses on whose complaints Mr. King is acting are:

Mary Church Terrell - for two terms a member of the D.C. Board of Education; chapter member, NAACP.

David H. Soul - member of the Society of Friends.

Rev. Arthur F. "Times" - Pastor of the Peoples Congregational Church.

Mrs. Easle Thomson - member of the United Caterers & Restaurant "Workers Union."

The witnesses were refused service at the Thompson cafeteria on Feb. 28, 1950 on the ground that three of the group are colored, by Mr. S. "Beeker, regional superintendent of the "Washington-Baltimore District of the Thompson chain.

According to a ruling of the corporation counsel, the law against discrimination for color in restaurants is now in effect, but no further prosecutions will be brought upon, although complaints will be accepted for further prosecution, until the courts have ruled on the Thompson case. Some restaurants have opened their doors since the district court ruling; however, several continue to refuse service. The "People's Restaurant Association, Inc., the corporation counsel, that the law is still in effect. The "Washington Restaurant Assn." has advised its members not to obey the law at present.

An amicus curiae brief will be filed by the Coordinating Committee for the Enforcement of the D.C. Anti-Discrimination Laws. This group is a committee after the D.C. chapter of the NAACP, Imadhers H. Dock	coutiously an oninion in May 1948 holding that the law has never been repealed, and has been concerned jointly vether being not into effect. The amicus brief reviews the changing and inconsistent official stand on the question of racial discrimination in the District of Columbia, and favors the enforcement of the law. Mrs. Margaret A. Haywood is the attorney representing the committee.

Because of the widespread interest in the case, it is expected that a number of other organizations will appear as friends of the court to file amicus briefs.

The John H. Thompson Co. is represented by Mr. Ringgold Hart, attorney for the Washington Restaurant Assn.

Mrs. Mary Church Terrell, chairman of the committee seeking enforcement of the laws stated:

"Those who are trying to enforce the anti-discrimination law here in Washington are rendering their country a great service. They are trying to stop it from being disregarded any longer by preventing hotels and restaurants from refusing to serve colored people in the capital of what is called the greatest democracy on earth!"
