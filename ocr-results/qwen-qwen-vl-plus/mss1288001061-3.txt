I think you would be sorry for my mother were you to see her now. Her body is weak, but her mental powers are hardly impaired. The care of her is overcast at times, for she is in the border land between sanity and insanity, and cannot yet be left to other hands than mine. She is quite an aged woman—twenty-five years old.

This beautiful, but far away, land—separated from all my friends, and from the many interests which have heretofore made my life. A more complete contrast to a large city can hardly be imagined than the surroundings here.

My youngest brother, an able fellow of intellectual verve, never very robust, became a physician. He had a brilliant professional future before him, when owing to mental strain and overwork, his health gave way. He fell a victim to that dreadful disease locomotor ataxia.

He cannot live in New York. The last winter we were in Madison Square was a very trying one in regard to weather, and a very sad one to us, as we were feeling deeply our recent bereavement in the sudden death of my oldest brother, our young wife, who had endeared herself to all our hearts in quite a remarkable manner.
