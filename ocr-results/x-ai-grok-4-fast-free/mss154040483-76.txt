Whose Government Is This? The Women

How to Get into the Cabinet by Mrs. Gall

There are two questions in the air to-day

of the League of Women Voters which have

been aired in the Bulletin for several months -

"If the women of the country find that they have

more than half the votes, what can they do

about it?" and "What about a woman in the

Cabinet?"

If every woman who belongs to the League

had saved her copy of the Bulletin which came

out last month she would have an answer to

the first, which was the subject of the most

thorough study the League has yet undertaken.

The survey was made after exhaustive study

of the extent to which women appear as voters,

as candidates, and officeholders, and the barriers

which prevent a full exercise of their political

power.  It was based upon the studies made by

the League branches in every state in the Union.

These studies show beyond question that women

do not have in their hands a majority of the

levers of government.

The extent to which they fail to use the vote

is even greater than the extent to which they

fail to win elective office.  A very considerable

proportion of the women who are entitled to

vote simply do not vote at all.  In some states

the proportion is close to 75 per cent.

What Can Be Done

What holds women back? The reasons for

their failure to vote in larger numbers are many

and complex.  Some of them are so deep-seated

that to change them would require social

changes so fundamental that they are beyond

practical politics.  Some of them, however,

can be directly attacked by political action.

It is pretty clear that one great obstacle is

the difficulty which the average woman finds

in getting to the polls.  This is most marked

in the cities.  It is the conclusion of the League's

study that a majority of the women who fail to

vote do so because of the physical difficulties

in the way.  In the country districts where it

is more easy for a woman to get to the polls,

her participation in elections is relatively

higher.

The most obvious remedy for this situation

is legislation providing for registration dates

which are less inconvenient, and legislation

forbidding elections on days when working

women cannot get away from their jobs.

Another practical suggestion made by the

League is that women should be organized in

small groups to provide transportation to the

polls.  The League itself might well bring

about a great increase in the number of women

voters by such activities.

What about getting into the higher brackets

of political life?  Here the difficulties are

even greater.  A member of the House who

has watched the development of the woman's

party from the days when it was known as the

National Woman's party said that the most

significant fact about women in politics is

that they do not seem to know that they have

any political power at all.  Nor does the

average man seem to be aware of it.  Women

do not vote as a body or as a group with

sufficient definiteness to make men fear their

displeasure.

Where We Stand

Where do we stand in elective office?  The

League survey has been thorough and the

figures with which it deals are complete up

to this time last year.  For the first time, it

has been possible to know the exact status of

women in political offices from the national

to the local unit.  For the first time, there is

a complete record of the places in which women

hold office, of the extent to which they are

represented in the various branches of the

government, and of the extent to which they

exercise the appointive as well as the elective

power.

The survey shows that in elective offices

there are, in round numbers, 20,000 women

officeholders in the United States.  This is

about one officeholder in every 5,000 of the

population.  In elective office alone, there are

more than 600,000 officers in the United

States, so that the number of women is

relatively small.

In appointive offices, the showing is better,

but still not large.  There are 3,000 women

holding national offices by appointment.  There

are 1,000 holding state offices.  In the

counties the number is 3,000, and in the cities

and towns, 5,000.

The offices held by women are to be found

in every branch of the government.  More than

half of all the women officeholders are clerks

or other employees of a subordinate character.

But there are women in the higher offices as

well.  There are three women holding federal

judgeships.  There was one woman in the

Cabinet, but she has now left it.  In the state

governments, there are 18 women holding

places as judges, and three as state librarians.

Three states now have women as superintendents

of public instruction and three more

have them as secretaries of state.

In Congress, there are two women senators

and nine representatives.  Most of the

elective offices held by women, however, are

in the state legislatures, where 129 women

hold seats.  In the larger cities, there are 26

women holding office as aldermen or councilmen.

What Is Needed

What is needed to make the government

more responsive to the needs and interests of

women?  There is general agreement among

students of the subject that the extension of

the appointive power to women is more

important even than their conquest of elective

office.  It will take a long time to change the

tradition that elective offices are for men,

and such important offices as those in the

Cabinet are still in the hands of the President

for appointment.

But pressure can be brought to bear to

increase the number of appointive offices

held by women.  It is pretty clear that the

average Congressman and the average state

legislator is more afraid of women organized

for political action than of individual women

acting singly.  The first essential, therefore,

is organization of women for political action.

The League of Women Voters is committed

to this program.  It believes that the education

of women voters is itself the most potent

form of political action.  It aims to prepare

women to exercise a deciding voice in all

public questions and to secure for women their

rightful place in the government.
