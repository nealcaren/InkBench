The struggle that had grown for four years would end so suddenly. But
the doors were opened, and when it began to rain, this new
life seemed ushered in. No one who has not seen it can imagine
the joy that filled every heart. Old men and women
wept for joy, and strong men embraced each other
and wept like children. The whole land seemed
to breathe freer, and the sun shone brighter.
We traveled on, and soon found ourselves in
North Carolina. The day the word “Lee Surrendered”
reached us there was great rejoicing.

CHAPTER XXXII

After Johnston’s Surrender at Raleigh,

Stanton’s army went to Greensboro. When it arrived at Raleigh, we
remained for some weeks, while he collected the paroled men
within the lines, in companies, and Ralph was commissioned with the
3d Regiment. During this time, many of us went to see Sherman
at night. I had Gen. Johnston’s permission to go with the others.
I told them I would stay, but
they insisted; so I went. We found him in a good
humor, and expecting to be mustered out in three or four
days. But the work in that region had been done so
thoroughly that no new regiments were needed to be raised.
This laudable task must be carried nobly. The end.
