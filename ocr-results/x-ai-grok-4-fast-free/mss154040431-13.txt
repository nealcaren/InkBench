INQUIRIES ABOUT PRESS BAN ON ABUSES

2. A. P. A. 2.

The committee appointed by the General Conference to inquire into the conduct of the press during the recent trials in Brooklyn, met in this city yesterday morning at 10 o'clock, in the Lecture Room of the Book Concern, Rev. Dr. John P. Durbin presiding. The committee consists of the following gentlemen: Rev. John P. Durbin, D.D., Rev. E. S. Janes, D.D., Rev. F. G. Hibbard, D.D., Rev. S. M. V. Hamilton, D.D., Rev. L. Swormstedt, and Mr. J. M. Walden.

The committee being organized, and after invoking the Divine direction and blessing, proceeded to business. The subject referred to them by the Conference was, "the conduct of the secular press in reference to the recent trials in the city of Brooklyn, and to report what action, if any, is necessary in the premises."

After considering the matter in all its bearings, the committee respectfully submit the following report:

They find that during the late trials in Brooklyn, much of the secular press throughout the country indulged in statements and comments which were calculated to injure the cause of religion and the interests of the Church. While they recognize the right of the press to report public proceedings, they deplore the sensationalism and license which characterized much of the reporting, and which tended to degrade the pulpit and to cast suspicion upon the ministerial character.

The committee believes that the Methodist Episcopal Church, through its official organ, the Christian Advocate, and other religious papers, maintained a dignified and Christian course, avoiding the excitements of the day, and confining themselves to such discussions as were consistent with the gravity of the subjects and the welfare of the Church.

In view of these facts, the committee recommend that the General Conference express its high appreciation of the course pursued by our own Church press, and that it urge upon all our members and friends the importance of supporting religious journalism as a counter influence to the secular press in times of public excitement.

They further recommend that no further action be deemed necessary in regard to the secular press, but that the Church rely upon the power of truth and the dissemination of sound religious literature to correct public morals.

Respectfully submitted,

John P. Durbin, Chairman.

The report was adopted by the committee, and ordered to be presented to the General Conference.
