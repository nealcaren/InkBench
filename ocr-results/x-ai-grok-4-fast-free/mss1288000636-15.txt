RICHMOND DISPATCH, SATURDAY MORNING, JANUARY 4, 1865

The Enemy on the Blackwater.

Petersburg, Jan. 3.

The enemy, 3,000 strong, came up the Blackwater to Ivor this morning, and attempted to cross the bridge there, but were driven back by a small force of our pickets, under Captain Whitworth.

Matters in East Tennessee.

By the Flag of Truce Boat.

A lady who came through the lines yesterday from Knoxville, says that the citizens in East Tennessee are suffering severely for food.  The people in the country, and especially in Sevier county, are very much disaffected towards the Yankees, and only await assistance to rise against them.  The conduct of the Yankee troops towards the people is outrageous.  They are robbing them of everything they can lay their hands upon.  The miller in Knoxville is compelled to grind for the Yankees without pay, and the farmers to haul grain to the Government without remuneration.  There is much suffering among the poor on account of the high prices.

From Northern Virginia.

Orange C. H., Jan. 2.

There is no change in the position of the two armies.  The Yankee cavalry have been very active, and a large scouting party came within five miles of this place yesterday, but finding our cavalry too strong for them, retired without making any attack.  The enemy's infantry are concentrated around Culpeper C. H., and show no signs of advancing.

The Weather.

The weather yesterday was warm and spring-like, with a slight fall of rain in the afternoon.

Lard -- 3.00.

The President and the Tennessee Campaign.

It will be seen by a telegraphic dispatch from Richmond that the President has issued an order relieving Gen. Hood from the command of the Army of Tennessee.  This action was no doubt taken in consequence of the failure of Gen. Hood's recent campaign in Tennessee.  Gen. Hood has displayed great bravery and energy, but his operations have not been attended with the success which was anticipated.

The Draft.

The drawing for the conscripts in this city, under the late call, commenced yesterday, and will be continued until completed.  A large number of names have been drawn, and the exempts are being examined.

Theatre.

Ford's Theatre -- "The Octoroon" -- A large and fashionable audience was in attendance last night at this popular place of amusement, to witness the performance of the beautiful and interesting drama of "The Octoroon."  The piece was admirably put upon the stage, and the acting throughout was of a high order.  Miss Keene, as Zoe, was particularly effective, and received frequent encores.  The house was well filled.

Amusements.

The Varieties -- "The Rivals" -- Sheridan’s comedy of "The Rivals" was produced at the Varieties last night to a crowded house.  The cast was an excellent one, and the piece went off with great spirit.  Mrs. Gillett, as Mrs. Malaprop, was inimitable, and Mr. Sothern, as Capt. Absolute, kept the audience in a roar of laughter.

Later from Sherman.

Augusta, Jan. 3.

The Constitutionalist has special dispatches from Savannah, dated yesterday morning, which say that our scouts report that Sherman has been reënforced by 20,000 men from Grant’s army, the whole of which reënforcements landed at Thunderbolt, near Savannah, all of yesterday.  Sherman immediately commenced moving his whole force in the direction of Augusta.  Wheeler is between him and this city, and Hardee on the Charleston and Savannah railroad.

Trying the Traitor.

The trial of the Yankee spy, Payne, who is charged with being engaged in the attempt to burn the city of New York, commenced in the United States Circuit Court at Washington on Monday.  The prisoner is defended by some of the ablest counsel in the District of Columbia.  The evidence against him is said to be overwhelming.

The case is attracting great attention, and the Court room is crowded daily with anxious spectators.

The Golden Wedding.

Mr. and Mrs. John Minor, of this city, celebrated the fiftieth anniversary of their marriage on Thursday evening.  A large number of their friends and relatives assembled to congratulate them upon the happy event.  The venerable couple, though far advanced in years, are both hale and hearty, and bid fair to see many more returns of the day.

Local Matters.

The Fire Department -- The annual election of officers of the Richmond Fire Department took place last night, and resulted in the choice of the following gentlemen: President, Wm. I. Clopton; Vice President, J. H. Bosher; Secretary, R. T. Harrison; Treasurer, J. W. Wright.

The Markets.

There was but little change in the markets yesterday.  Provisions continue scarce and high.

This and That.

Mr. Editor:  Will you allow me space in your columns to say a word in behalf of the poor soldiers in our hospitals?  Many of them are suffering for the want of proper bedding and clothing.  Cannot the ladies of Richmond devise some means to supply this want?  A. B. C.

Amusements.

Belasco's Varieties -- A star engagement of M'lle Agoust, the great French equestrienne, commences at this theatre to-night.  Her feats of horsemanship are truly wonderful, and she is said to be the most graceful and daring rider before the public.  The performance will conclude with a laughable afterpiece.

Continue of Local Matters.

The condition of the streets is truly horrible.  The mud is ankle deep in many places, and the authorities should take immediate steps to have them repaired.

The Small-Pox.

There are now ten cases of small-pox in the city, all of which are under treatment at the Small-Pox Hospital.  The disease is of a mild type, and no deaths have occurred.

From the North.

Washington, Jan. 3.

The Herald's special says that Grant's army is still inactive.  There is no indication of any immediate movement.

New York, Jan. 3.

The steamship Arago, from Charleston on the 30th ult., has arrived.  She brings no news of importance.

The weather is clear and cold.  The mercury at daylight stood at 10 degrees above zero.

The Gold Market.
