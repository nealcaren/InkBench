Washington. Slavery.

We, the undersigned, members of the colored race, of the city of Washington, do most solemnly protest against the passage of the bill now pending before Congress, and known as the “Fugitive Slave” bill.  It is after mature deliberation, and with a deliberate conviction of its evil tendencies, that we make this protest.  It is not that we are insensible to the wrongs which, under the Constitution, we are doomed to endure, but from a desire to save from further degradation the few who remain in bondage.  We feel that the passage of this bill would be a virtual surrender of the rights of the colored citizen, and a triumph to the slave power.

We know that the Constitution, as it now stands, is sufficiently pro-slavery, without the addition of this odious measure, which would make every man a slave-catcher, and every house a prison.  We know, too, that the spirit of liberty is abroad in the land, and that the people are becoming more and more opposed to slavery.  Why, then, should Congress pass this bill, which would array the whole North against the South, and tend to perpetuate the very evil it pretends to suppress?

We appeal to the Senators and Representatives from the free States, to vote against this bill.  We appeal to them in the name of humanity, in the name of justice, and in the name of the God who made us all of one blood.  We ask them to remember that we are men and brethren, and that the chains which bind one bind all.

We know that there are those who say that this bill is necessary to carry out the provisions of the Constitution.  But we deny that the Constitution requires any such legislation.  The clause in the Constitution relating to fugitives from service is a compact between the States, and should be carried out in a spirit of mutual concession and compromise.  But this bill goes beyond the Constitution, and makes the free States the hunting-ground for slaves.

We protest against it, because it legalizes kidnapping, and makes every Northern man a slave-hunter.  We protest against it, because it strikes at the root of our liberty, and endangers the safety of every colored man in the land.  We protest against it, because it is a blow at the humane spirit of the age, and a reproach to the American name.

For these reasons, we earnestly implore Congress to reject this bill, and to leave the Constitution as it is, without this infamous addition.

John Cook,
Washington, D. C., June 10th, 1850.
