Upon the high seas (the highways of nations) a right  
which has been uniformly understood, -- and since the  
creation of the constitution, has been practically  
recognized and enforced, -- is necessarily brought up  
before the supreme court.  The decision in this  
case will, undoubtedly, be given upon the  
property of the vessel and freight.  The decision  
of the jury was against the government, and the  
decision of the supreme court is not final in matters  
of internal revenue.  

Some persons have supposed that this clause  
in the constitution, concerning the power to limit  
or to forbid the increase or diminution of the salaries  
of the judges, was intended to guard against the  
appointment of men of depraved character to the  
chief magistracy, which the laws of the several states  
cannot control, and keeping them from the extreme  
of degeneracy.  And this, while it may be true, does not  
relieve the courts of war, suspending which the house  
of lords exercises a veto power, and superseding  
which the people elect their own chief magistrate.  
The purpose of the constitution in this particular, is  
to secure the independence of the judiciary from the  
influence of the executive and legislative departments.  
The clause providing that "the judges, both of the  
supreme and inferior courts, shall hold their offices  
during good behavior," was intended to secure this  
independence.  And this, while it may be true, does not  
relieve the courts of the necessity of deciding cases  
in accordance with the law, and not against it.  
The constitution is the supreme law of the land, and the judges are sworn to support it.
