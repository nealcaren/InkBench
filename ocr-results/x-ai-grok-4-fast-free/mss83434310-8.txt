No. 1.

Two of the five Miller boys were in the fight and came out without a scratch. This at
least is more than they could say of their enemies. We had one man killed and two wounded.
Jno. S. Miller—this is the report from which Fred. Miller got his information. The “Sun”
published the story of Priv. Fred. Miller. It was all in the “Dispatch” first.
Jno. S. Miller says Belle Isle was the place where they were incarcerated. Of the five, all were re-
leased promptly except one, who was detained on suspicion of being a spy.
The attack upon the train took place about three miles from Beaver Dam, and was made by Gen.
Kautz’s cavalry, reinforced by some infantry dismounted. The fight was a very gallant one, and result-
ed in the capture of the train and about 300 prisoners.

Not guilty.

No. 2. Private John Miller, Co. M, 6th Md. Cavalry—

“Guardi” in the “Dispatch” gave the story of the fight near Beaver Dam.
The attack was made on the train, probably by Kautz’s cavalry, and was a very gallant fight, re-
sulting in the capture of the train and 70 or 80 prisoners.  Not guilty.

No. 3. Private George Miller, Co. H, New Orleans Volunteers—

Says—“A man came to me in New Orleans last July and asked me to enlist in the army. He
said I should have plenty to eat and good clothing, and all that I would want. I had only one suit
of clothes left, and so I went to the provost marshal’s and enlisted in the 8th New York Heavy Artil-
lery, for three years, or during the war.
The train was going to Richmond with prisoners, and was attacked about seven miles from Bea-
ver Dam by a party of cavalry. The train was saved by the guard firing out of the windows.

Not guilty.
