The Freedmen's Bureau

The friends of the colored race for a year past have
been investigating the subject, and it would seem that
while they have discovered much to condemn in the
manner in which the act has been executed by the Bureau,
they have also found enough in the same subject to
excuse the Bureau from the severe censure which it has
sometimes received.

The Constitution has itself been made the subject of much
inquiry in official circles, and it is now proposed by the Chief
officer of the Bureau to reduce to writing the breaking up of the
old slave states system.  The Chief has left the work
of the internal trade to the best of his ability, and will hereafter
bend his energies to the work of
executing the laws; and the old slave system as
embedded in the laws will be rooted up.
Every officer under the Bureau will be required to have
the instructions numbered if they come from the General
who has done so much for the colored race in this District.
His orders as to the pay of the scouts, will be of the first
importance, and will be executed by all officers.
The provisions of the law as to the payment of the army
and the navy, and the payment of the pensions or the
soldiers who have fought for the Union, will be carried out
by all officers under his command, who are required to do
their duty in this respect.
In all of this the policy of the administration will be
carried out, and the rights of the colored race will be
protected.

The letter for which you have waited so long is at last
written, and I hope it will give you much pleasure.  I
know that you have been anxious for it, and I have done
my best to make it satisfactory.  The letter is for the use
of the Bureau, and I trust it will be of service in your
department.  I have enclosed a copy for your files, and
shall be glad if you will use it in any way that may be
useful to the cause of freedom.  The letter is written in
the spirit of the law, and I trust it will be of use in
promoting the interests of the colored people.

The friends of the colored race have been much
exercised on the subject of the Freedmen's Bureau, and
the manner in which it has been conducted.  They have
made many inquiries, and have sought information from
every quarter.  They have examined the reports of the
officers, and have compared them with the facts as they
have come to their knowledge.  The result of their
investigations has been to convince them that there is
much to commend in the management of the Bureau, and
that the officers have done their duty faithfully and well.
The friends of the colored race will continue to watch the
operations of the Bureau, and will do all in their power to
aid it in the accomplishment of its great work.
