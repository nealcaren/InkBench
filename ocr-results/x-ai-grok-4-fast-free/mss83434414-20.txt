They were advised to make application for the positions in the Medical Staff of the Department of the South.

The applications of the latter two named applicants were also informally refused on account of the superabundance of medical officers in the army generally.  They were advised to seek positions in the hospitals of Washington until the necessities of the service abroad should require their services.  The applicant whose name appears last, being an Assistant Surgeon in the army, was advised to apply to the Medical Director of the Department of the South for the position he desired.

Owing to the organization of the Bureau not being fully perfected until July 25th, 1864, one months [sic] delay in replying to these applications was unavoidable.  A general circular letter, stating the above, and advising applicants to seek employment in the hospitals at Washington until the organization of the medical staff of the Bureau was perfected, was sent to all the above named applicants on the 1st of August, 1864.

A colored Assistant Surgeon, Dr. J. DeGrasse, was appointed and assigned to duty at the Freedmen's Hospital, Washington, D.C., on the 1st of August, 1864.

The whole number of applications received for medical appointments in the Bureau to Aug. 31st, 1864, was 62, of which 9 were colored applicants.  Of these, 4 have been appointed and assigned to duty, viz: three white and one colored.

Since Aug. 31st, 1864, seven more applications have been received, of which two were from colored applicants, none of which have been acted upon.  In addition to these, 15 applications from colored physicians have been received and filed, to be considered when suitable vacancies occur.

The above embraces the condition of the medical appointments in the Bureau to Sept. 15th, 1864.
