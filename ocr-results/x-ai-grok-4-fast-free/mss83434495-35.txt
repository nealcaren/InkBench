CHAPTER XI.

The Yorktown Sesquicentennial.

On the one hundredth anniversary of the surrender of Cornwallis at Yorktown in October, 1781, the Nation celebrated the victory which ended the Revolutionary War and secured our independence. The celebration was nation-wide.

Along the Valley rail from Pittsburgh I went west to Washington, seeing at night sights which were beautiful and impressive. At Philadelphia to the night when the first night’s ride, a procession of twenty thousand illuminated the streets. Behind their banners, more than a hundred girls, of eighteen or twenty, in white, with Washington wreaths, sang the hymn, “God Save Our Country.”

From the White House I went south to Richmond, where the Pennsylvania and West India Ports were located in their homes. Of immeasurable importance: during the French campaigns, and the first of the twenty-four hours, we were all the way to Washington, and the White House was the first to be the first.

It was in May of the year 1796 that the capital of the United States first met here in this city, and was to stay to Washington from then on.
