Alice Stone Blackwell

Alice Stone Blackwell

daughter of Lucy Stone & Henry B. Blackwell, was born Aug. 14, 1857. Her mother was a leader in the woman's rights movement, and Alice, from earliest childhood, was immersed in the agitation for woman's suffrage. The movement was then in its infancy, and her parents lived and breathed it. Her father and mother gave their full time and energy to it, and the child absorbed it through every pore.

She was educated at Boston University, and for several years after she graduated, she taught school. In 1881 she joined the editorial staff of the Woman's Journal, the official organ of the National American Woman Suffrage Association, of which her parents were president, and of which she herself later became president. She held this position until 1917, when the Journal was merged with the Woman Citizen. During these thirty-six years she was associate editor and later editor of the paper, which under her father's editorship had been carrying on a crusade for woman's rights since 1870.

The Woman's Journal was then the only weekly paper devoted entirely to woman's rights, and it had a circulation of over 20,000, reaching every state in the Union. It was non-partisan, and advocated the election of women to office, equal pay for equal work, and co-education. Miss Blackwell contributed to many other periodicals, and was a frequent speaker at suffrage conventions and on the platform.

In 1890 she became secretary of the Massachusetts Woman Suffrage Association, and held that office for several years. She was also active in the work of the New England Woman Suffrage Association, and later in the National Association. She was a delegate to many international conferences on suffrage, and was one of the organizers of the International Woman Suffrage Alliance.

Her literary work includes poems, stories, and translations from Russian and other languages. She edited her mother's letters and biography, and wrote "Lucy Stone: Pioneer of Woman's Rights," published in 1930. She was also the author of several pamphlets on suffrage.

Miss Blackwell was a lifelong pacifist, and during the World War she was active in the Woman's Peace Party. She was a member of the American Friends Service Committee, and worked for the relief of war sufferers.

Alice Stone Blackwell died March 15, 1950, at the age of ninety-two. She had devoted her life to the cause of woman suffrage, and to the advancement of peace and justice.
