LAST WEEK the Ford Foundation announced the appointment of a new chairman, McGeorge Bundy, 47, former Special Assistant to the President for National Security Affairs. Bundy succeeds Henry T. Heald, 60, who is retiring after five years as president of the world's largest private philanthropy.  

The foundation, which has assets of more than $3,000,000,000 and an annual budget of about $200,000,000, has been under fire from both liberals and conservatives. Liberals have criticized it for not doing enough for the poor and minorities; conservatives have charged that it is too liberal in its grant policies. Bundy's appointment is seen as an attempt to steer a middle course. Bundy, who was president of the Ford Foundation from 1966 to 1979, faced the challenge of maintaining the foundation's independence while responding to social needs.  

In recent years, the foundation has focused increasingly on urban problems, education, and international development. Under Bundy, it launched major initiatives in these areas, including support for public television, legal aid for the poor, and efforts to improve race relations.  

But the foundation's future direction remains uncertain. With the U.S. facing deepening social divisions and economic challenges, the Ford Foundation is at a crossroads, deciding how to allocate its vast resources in an era of government cutbacks and private philanthropy scrutiny.  

VIET NAM  

U.S. Planes Bomb Hanoi Area  

For the first time in the war, U.S. planes struck targets in the heart of North Viet Nam last week. In a series of raids, American aircraft bombed military installations near Hanoi, including an ammunition dump and a radio station. The raids were in retaliation for North Vietnamese attacks on U.S. ships in the Gulf of Tonkin.  

The bombing escalated the conflict, drawing international condemnation. The Soviet Union and Communist China denounced the U.S. actions, while South Viet Nam and its allies praised the moves as necessary to halt aggression. President Johnson emphasized that the raids were limited and aimed at military targets, not civilians.  

On the ground, U.S. and South Vietnamese forces continued operations in the south, battling Viet Cong guerrillas in the Mekong Delta and central highlands. Casualties mounted on both sides, with no clear strategic advantage emerging.  

THE WORLD  

Pope Paul in the U.S.  

Pope Paul VI arrived in New York last week for a brief but historic visit—the first by a reigning pontiff to the Western Hemisphere. Amid tight security and cheering crowds, he addressed the United Nations General Assembly, pleading for peace in Viet Nam and the Middle East.  

"Peace, peace!" he cried, calling on world leaders to end the arms race and seek dialogue over destruction. The speech was hailed as a moral high point for the U.N., though it offered no concrete proposals.  

Later, the Pope celebrated Mass at Yankee Stadium before 90,000 faithful, blessing the city and urging Catholics to live by Gospel values in a secular age. His visit, though short, symbolized the Church's outreach to modern society.  

BUSINESS  

Auto Strike Looms  

Tensions rose in Detroit last week as United Auto Workers and the Big Three automakers neared a deadline for a new contract. With 600,000 workers poised to walk out, demands for higher wages, better pensions, and safety improvements clashed with management concerns over costs and foreign competition.  

Negotiations stalled over issues like cost-of-living adjustments and plant relocation clauses. Industry analysts warned that a strike could disrupt production lines and idle suppliers, potentially costing the economy millions daily. As the October 1 expiration loomed, both sides dug in, raising fears of the first major auto strike in years.  

MEDICINE  

New Hope for Heart Patients  

Researchers announced promising results from a new drug that could revolutionize treatment for congestive heart failure. Tested on hundreds of patients, the compound digoxin improved cardiac function and reduced symptoms in 70% of cases, with fewer side effects than traditional therapies.  

Developed at a major university hospital, the drug works by strengthening heart muscle contractions without raising blood pressure dangerously. While not a cure, it offers hope for millions suffering from the disease, which kills more Americans than any other. Clinical trials continue, with FDA approval expected within two years.  

SHOW BUSINESS  

Elizabeth Taylor and Richard Burton  

Hollywood's golden couple, Elizabeth Taylor and Richard Burton, announced plans for their next film, a lavish adaptation of Shakespeare's "Antony and Cleopatra." Filming is set to begin in Rome, with the stars reuniting after the success of "Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf?"  

The project, budgeted at $10 million, promises spectacle with battles on the Nile and opulent sets. Off-screen, Taylor and Burton's stormy romance continues to captivate tabloids, blending high drama with their real-life passion. Critics wonder if the film can match their onstage chemistry.  

SPORTS  

World Series Preview  

As October approached, excitement built for the 1966 World Series between the Baltimore Orioles and Los Angeles Dodgers. The Orioles, powered by Brooks Robinson's defense and Frank Robinson's bat, had dominated the American League, winning 97 games.  

The Dodgers, defending champions, relied on Sandy Koufax's pitching wizardry and a balanced lineup led by Maury Wills' speed. Key matchups include Koufax vs. Orioles' sluggers, promising a clash of styles: finesse vs. power. Fans anticipated a classic seven-game series.
