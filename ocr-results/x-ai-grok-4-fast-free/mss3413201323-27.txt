Letters from Monroe.

Monroe, Iowa, May 10,—Editor Dial: I have just read your last issue of the Dial, and was much pleased with the article from the pen of our townsman, Mr. John P. Irish, on the subject of woman's suffrage. It is a timely article, and one that should be read by every voter in the land. Mr. Irish has long been known as a friend to the cause of woman, and his words carry weight wherever they are heard. We here in Monroe remember well the days when he stood before the public and advocated the rights of women to the ballot. It was back in the year 1870, during the agitation following the 15th amendment, that Mr. Irish made one of his most notable speeches in favor of female suffrage. He was then editor of the Iowa State Register, and his paper was foremost in championing the cause.

When John P. Irish Favored Suffrage.

In the stirring times of 1870, when the question of woman's right to vote was being hotly debated throughout the country, John P. Irish, now a prominent citizen of this city, was one of the foremost champions of the cause in Iowa. At that time he was editor of the Iowa State Register, published at Des Moines, and his editorials and public addresses did much to advance the movement in the northwest. The 15th amendment to the federal constitution, granting suffrage to colored men, had just been adopted, and this action, while hailed by many as a great step forward, was regarded by the advocates of woman's suffrage as a bitter disappointment, inasmuch as it ignored the claims of women altogether. The amendment was made to read: "The right of citizens of the United States to vote shall not be denied or abridged by the United States or by any state on account of race, color or previous condition of servitude." The exclusion of sex from the enumeration of disqualifications was a severe blow to those who had hoped that the reconstruction period would witness the enfranchisement of women.

It was under these circumstances that Mr. Irish entered the lists in behalf of woman suffrage. In a powerful editorial in the Register of March 15, 1870, he said: "If the ballot is a right, it is the right of every human being capable of exercising it intelligently, without regard to sex. To deny it to woman is to perpetuate an injustice that has no parallel in the history of oppression. Let us have justice for all, and then we shall have peace."

This utterance aroused a storm of discussion. The conservative element in the republican party, to which Mr. Irish then belonged, was opposed to any extension of the suffrage at that time, fearing it would weaken the party in the south; but Mr. Irish was not to be silenced. He followed up his editorial with a speech at the state convention of the Iowa Woman Suffrage association, held in Des Moines in April of that year. His address, entitled "The Logic of the Ballot," is still remembered by those who heard it as one of the most eloquent pleas ever made for the cause in Iowa.

"The ballot," said Mr. Irish, "is not a privilege to be doled out sparingly by those in power; it is a weapon of defense and a shield of liberty for the weak against the strong. Woman, who bears the burdens of the home and rears the citizens of the republic, is entitled to a voice in the government she helps to sustain. To withhold it is not only unjust, but dangerous to the state itself. The time has come when this anomaly must be swept away."

The convention adopted resolutions endorsing Mr. Irish's views, and his speech was widely circulated in pamphlet form. It contributed materially to the passage by the Iowa legislature in 1870 of a bill giving women the right to vote in school elections—a partial victory that paved the way for further gains.

Mr. Irish's advocacy of suffrage did not end with that year. Throughout the decade he continued to support the movement, both in his paper and on the platform. In 1873 he delivered an address at the national convention in Washington, where he shared the stage with such leaders as Susan B. Anthony and Elizabeth Cady Stanton. "I am here," he said, "not as a politician seeking votes, but as a citizen demanding justice. The west, with its broad prairies and free institutions, should lead the van in this reform."

When Mr. Irish removed to California some years later, he brought with him his enthusiasm for the cause, and though his active participation has been less prominent of late, owing to other duties, his early efforts are still gratefully remembered by the suffrage workers of the state. It is fitting that his views should be recalled at this time, when the question is again coming to the front in California politics.

The Late Storm.

The heavy rain of last week did considerable damage to the fruit crops in the valley districts. Reports from Riverside indicate that many orange groves were washed out, and the loss to growers will aggregate thousands of dollars. In Los Angeles the streets were flooded, but no serious injury resulted to property.
