It has been proposed, that by the use of the words "of the United
States," the law and government of each State of the Union
would be bound by it. But it is plain that the laws of the Union
apply to every State independently of the will of the State govern-
ments. The word "States" here is used in the plural number, and
the proposition relates to the powers of Congress in relation to
the United States as a nation.
It has been further proposed, that this subject should be introduced into
the Constitution, to wit, the power of Congress to commission officers
of the army and navy. It is not subject to any such objection.
Chap. 4. sect. 1. and 2. of the 1st. vol. of the Laws of the U States, passed
at Philadelphia, Dec. 22, 1793. The power of Congress to commission
officers of the army, and navy, is one of the powers of the United
States, which, when the army and navy are employed, is to be
exercised by Congress, and not by the President. The power of
appointing officers is necessarily incidental to the power of
raising armies and equipping vessels of war; but before the
President can commission officers, the army and navy must be
raised and equipped by Congress. In the case of an invasion or
insurrection, the President may call forth the militia, and when so
called, he may appoint officers; but the commissions which he issues
are temporary, and expire with the occasion.
The whole subject of the military establishment is placed
under the control of Congress, and the President is the
commander in chief. The power to declare war, and to regulate
captures, is vested in Congress, and the President has only the
executive power over the forces when raised and employed.
