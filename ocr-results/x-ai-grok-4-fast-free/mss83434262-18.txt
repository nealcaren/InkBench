FUNDS FOR WIDOWS

COLLECTING MONEY FOR WIDOWS

Towards the close of the year 1807, the merchants of the city of New York had deposited in their stores and elsewhere of flour and provisions of all sorts, to the amount of two millions of dollars, as near as could be ascertained; and it was supposed that there were such of breadstuffs and of other articles of the first necessity, as would supply the market for six months to come, without any new importation. But the Embargo, which was passed a few days after, frustrated, in a great measure, their scheme. It rendered their property of no immediate value, and exposed them to the losses to which they are now subject, and which many of them are now suffering.

FROM THE NEW YORK SPECTATOR:

An inspection of the goods left on hand in New York, is marked with singular interest. Over twelve thousand hogsheads of tobacco, or estimated the worth of $200,000, are said to be on hand in this city alone. The losses of the merchants, who imported these articles from England last year, are past calculation. The books of the merchants, show that property to the amount of three millions of dollars, shipped from Liverpool in 1807, still remains in this port unsold.

List &c of a Report: The goods were packed in casks or bales, and sent to the care of consignees; the number amounting to four or five vessels, of this description of property.
