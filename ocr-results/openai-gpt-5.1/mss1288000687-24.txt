NEW ENGLAND PORTRAITS  
ALICE STONE BLACKWELL  

An undaunted devotion to the cause of woman suffrage links the name of Alice Stone Blackwell, counselor of the Boston League of Women Voters, with that of her famous mother, Lucy Stone, pioneer of woman’s rights. Her father, Henry B. Blackwell, was also an ardent and practically the only man who really devoted his life to obtaining equal rights for women. One of Miss Blackwell’s aunts, Elizabeth Blackwell, was the first woman to take a medical degree, and another, Antoinette Brown Blackwell, was the first woman to be ordained a minister. Miss Blackwell was graduated from Boston University in 1881, and then joined her parents in working for woman suffrage and in editing the Woman’s Journal, carrying the paper on alone after their death until 1917 when it became the Woman Citizen. For many years she appeared annually before the committee of the Massachusetts Legislature to answer the anti-suffrage arguments, and a distinguished lawyer once said he considered he had never heard abler presentations of controversial matters brought together there. It was also Miss Blackwell who brought together the two parties of the suffrage movement, when their estrangement weakened their effectiveness, welding them in 1890 into the National American Woman Suffrage Association, of which she was secretary for twenty years. For years she has been on the board of trustees of Boston University and a member of Phi Beta Kappa. Close to her heart is the cause of the Armenians. She has translated into English a volume of Armenian poems, as well as one of Russian, one of Hungarian, one of Yiddish and one, still unpublished, of Mexican poetry. She is the American editor for the biography of Catherine Breshkovsky, “The Little Grandmother.” Her biography of Catherine Breshkovsky, “The Little Grandmother,” the “best known woman of the Russian Revolution,” was at one time one of the six best sellers.
