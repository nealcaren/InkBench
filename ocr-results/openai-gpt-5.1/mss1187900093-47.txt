then I said “I will write” and
this is my letter of inquiry.
Can we give up your
journey to Hayti? If so I
cannot help being glad, as there
are a good many reasons why
I had rather think of you
both as nearer to us. If I could
hear written as formerly, I should
not have so long postponed my
inquiries. But I am oblige
to curtail my letters both as to
number & length.
I hope that it will turn
out that you are to pass the
Winter in your pleasant and
comfortable home. Why should
you brave the terrors of the
reging sea and many inconveniences
when you are so well arranged
in your quiet and delightful
home? Not for fame or gain have

we already yours Both of you—
I was glad to see a notice
of your lecture and wished that
I was there to enjoy it. I’m
sure that you can make your
experiences very interesting and
valuable. And then Mrs.
Douglass has I doubt not her
hands full of literary work,
alot of which you can assist
him if you have not all that
you can do yourself.
I shall write to Miss Gradner
to-day and tell her all that
I know of your movements,
because, ’tis much I am certain
that you cannot lecture in
Washington and be also on the sea.
Her “pathes” are mine.
I want Mr. Douglass to avoid
great mental strain, and to linger
his waning years, that he
may stay as long as possible with us.
