Controlling Groups  

A small group of people determined  
to oppose promised legislation, if pro-  
vided with money, as were the brewers  
and distillers, may hold back legislation  
and prevent action being taken, even  
though the masses of the people demand  
action. What the wets did to the suf-  
frage movement other groups of people  
have done and may do for other great  
causes. Such groups of people may  
make controlling contributions to politi-  
cal parties and in return secure promises  
of no action in legislatures or Congress,  
provided that party is elected to power.  
It may secure the orders of that party,  
all the way down the line to the most  
remote rural county, instruct chairman,  
to defeat a referendum providing for  
any measure.  

Who were the railroads fighting rati-  
fication in Tennessee? Certainly not  
because the railroads cared whether  
women voted or not. Why was the  
Manufacturers' Association there to  
work against ratification? It was be-  
cause these interests were disconnected,  
combined that the lobby help the pro-

gram when necessary arose. The com-

has happened over and over again.  
Presumably the program was that of the  
liquor interests, but many other interests  
under it as support.  

There is also the autocracy of what  
is known as the boss system to be  
watched. In one of the states which did  
not ratify there were six men capable  
of being owned absolutely by a political

I have always believed that because there

A system of this country for taking

It has been from decade to decade much

revolution in the methods of taking the  
vote, getting the nominations, making

Here is the address made by Mrs. Catt  
at the Cleveland Convention of the  
League of Women Voters when, at her  
suggestion, the Committee on Election  
Laws and Methods was elevated to a de-  
partment of work called Efficiency in

so fine an expression of the failure of  
our governmental machinery to register  
the people's wishes that it has been re-  
peatedly asked for by our readers.  

There were two questions only in  
that campaign and there were many  
others. So difficult was it for women to  
register the opinions they wanted to ex-  
press in that election that very many  
of them felt the vote would here of  
any value to them. What happened  
then, happens all the time, because there  
is a conflict between the state and the  
nation and because political machines  
and bosses do so much political juggling  
over issues.  

Reform, therefore, is needed all along  
the line. It is needed in the election of  
the President of the United States and  
all the way down to the dog-catcher in  
the smallest village.  

Where We Need Reform  

Perhaps we need it most in our plat-  
form making. Ours is a country gov-  
erned by parties. Parties are of tremen-  
dous power in this land, so tremendous  
that sooner or later the bills of a  
referendum submitted by the legislature  
may not only be rejected, the candidates  
of the parties whose platforms have dis-  
obedient electors walk up to the polls  
and rat or adopt the measure. Plat-  
forms are made not for the people but  
by those who have interest in them and  
those are inclined in nothing to say.  
They have little or no opportunity to  
tell the party officials. Consequently no  
ally is relied upon to carry the ticket  
based upon the platforms and party  
loyalty usually successfully accom-  
plishes the aims set by the managers.  

Progress is being made toward re-  
forming our present absurd election sys-  
tem, but we are traveling toward re-  
form in what seems to be the fanciest lead-  
ing not only the methods of registering  
but not an uncommon one of the  
vote registering of the opinions of those  
for the silent majority who do not  
cast ballots. It is an absurd and impos-  
sible practice that our elections should  
not be made educational that the people  
should not be able to cast an intelli-  
gent ballot, that parties properly to rep-  
resent the issues at stake.  

Therefore the Committee on Election  
Laws and Methods recommended that  
this special committee should be dropped  
and that Efficiency in Government, which  
had been created by merging it with  
Laws and Methods, be made a depart-  
ment of the National League of  
Women Voters. That is, the League may  
always manage and emphasize political  
conduct.  

There are three main reasons for this  
proposed change:  

First: We were a political body when  
we were working for the suffrage and  
we secured the vote by political action.
