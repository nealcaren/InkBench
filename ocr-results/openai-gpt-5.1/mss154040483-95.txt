“Party Clean‑ups Are  
the Crying Need”  

Says  

Carrie Chapman Catt  

Distinguished Suffrage Leader  

[photograph]  

Mrs. Catt                                 Bachrach  


WANTED, feminism in politics. Emily Newell Blair says so. Mrs. Blair, sunny‑tempered, broad‑visioned and free from literal mindedness, confesses herself (in her recent journal articles) to be pessimistic and discouraged about women in politics. Her conclusions are drawn from seven years’ experience and hard work as vice‑chairman of the National Democratic Committee.  

She admits that when women sit in conventions and on committees, write constitutions, adopt platforms and support candidates, they have done so with credit to themselves and benefit to their parties, but she charges that the machine which makes up the tickets and manages the campaigns is still in the hands of men; that women are not permitted any real share in that control and that consequently the feminine point of view does not get itself expressed in party policies and party action. Mrs. Blair finds that women have been kept in the party work at the bottom instead of the top.  

But she says there is a twin control of politics. “On the one hand we have the two national committees and the state and county committees,” she says in effect, “and on the other we have the organization which raises and expends the vast party funds, doles out the patronage and controls the contracts and the spoils. The women have been allowed to come into the first, but the second, which is the real control, has remained almost wholly in the hands of men.”  

Mrs. Blair suggests that the remedy is the application of more feminism. I agree. She is more tolerant of the other party than I am. Mrs. Blair says that there are two ways only in which women may become a power in politics and those are by being either in the control of the party machine, or by being better voters than the men. One is not in their power at all for some time to come. The other is in full for immediate effort.  

We are living in a revival of moral issues in politics. Is it a brand‑new thing that we have found politics so saturated with immorality, graft and fraud? Mrs. Blair may think it is. If so, I do not think her diagnosis correct.  

The fact is that when the women of the western world walked through the open door into politics, they went into a system already rotten with four hundred years of party corruption. There had been sporadic efforts at reform. Now and then a new party, disgusted with the old abuses, arose and tried to clean house, but the entrenched system absorbed it or destroyed it. The new voters did not know this history, and it was not altogether their fault that they did not. They were told that politics was a noble profession, and they believed it.  

So, when Mrs. Blair says that the same kind of thing that has happened in one party has also occurred in the others, she is right.  

Mrs. Blair suggests that the remedy is in the application of more feminism. I believe this is the clearest statement of the situation that has yet been made.  

When women first came into politics they were told that they were to use “influence,” and not “organization.” They were to coax and persuade and plead and never to threaten. It is time for a change of tactics. I believe that the only way in which women can now bring about the cleansing of politics is by using force at the polls, in colonizing and in distributing patronage to whom patronage is due, in all the frauds and tricks that go to make up the worst form of practical politics, the men who now control state and national governments were always our equals and often our masters.  

So the years passed. Votes were bought, nominations were controlled by secret, log‑rolling organizations; political strategy, partisan gerrymandering, pressure on voters, intimidation of employes, were practiced until the point is often less known by what it must overcome than from the price at which a nomination is sold.  

When a distinguished politician in New York, a Republican machine‑chief in Boston, and Philadelphia, which is every where a synonym for machine control, used without protest the very methods which once were denounced by the party in power as infamous, when a minister of the gospel could be found willing to defend the use of such methods as “practical politics,” it was time that somebody should speak out.  

Women must realize that in every purchased office, must be recognized by all voters as a political crime and political honesty must be made the common standard of all parties before any simple remedy can be applied effectively.

every purchased office, must be recognized by all voters as a political crime and political honesty must be made the common standard of all parties before any simple remedy can be applied effectively.  

What we cry from women of all parties is that they will no longer support a party that profits on fraudulent returns, guarantees profit on certain lines of graft, assures the reelection of men to office who have been convicted in the courts, and will not protect a judge who, being honest, would protect their right to vote.  

If women knew that they hold in their own hands the power to make and unmake parties, if they could understand the tremendous effect of working inside and not outside the party machines, then the moral issue in politics would be met.  

Suppose Mrs. Blair and her friends, and all other women of all parties, should say:  

Are we honest?  

Are we sound?  

Are we determined to accomplish all that democracy implies?  

The big parties call for more feminism, and the little ones for more democracy; then, when honesty has been recognized, where? The door of the Round Table will be set wide.  

“Shall we have the next question: How shall the result wanted be attained?”
