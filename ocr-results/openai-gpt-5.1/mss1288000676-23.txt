ALICE STONE BLACKWELL  

Alice Stone Blackwell, more than any other living person, symbolizes  
the whole range of the struggle of women through two generations to win untrammeled  
human status.  One of her aunts was the first woman in the world to obtain a medical  
degree and another aunt was the first to be ordained a minister.  Her mother, Lucy  
Stone, was the first Massachusetts woman to go to college, became a lecturer against  
Negro slavery and for woman’s rights when mere public speaking by women was  
considered an indecency, and throughout her life was one of the half-dozen great  
national figures in the woman’s movement.  Her father gave a fortune in service to  
the woman’s cause.  

The life of the daughter was inextricably interwoven from childhood in the widely  
varied activities of the reform in which Miss Blackwell has recorded in her book “Lucy  
Stone, Pioneer of Woman’s Rights.”  Miss Blackwell received her A.B. and A.M. from  
Boston University in her early youth.  Her work in suffrage began with joining the  
Woman Suffrage Association for about forty years; and President of the New England  
and the Massachusetts Woman Suffrage Associations.  As Miss Blackwell’s chief  
weapon was the pen, one of her many tasks was editing the Woman’s Journal as  
much as any other campaigner.  But her work of editing the Women’s Journal  
and writing for that paper, as well as innumerable leaflets, articles, newspaper letters  
and other published material furnished the money which was put into the propaganda  
of information, lobbying and the countless spirit of friends.  This single soul was  
an editor of outstanding ability.  In the council chamber she applied her rich wisdom,  
strong opinions, rare foresight, and sane questing spirit to weigh out its strategy which  
it was her particular and life’s guidance to tip with the final judgment of her  
righteous dealing.  

The instant the ballot was won she took up the task of the League of Woman Voters  
and the newer issues of bio-political citizenship.  She became Honorary Chairman and  
has since helped to create and shape the whole League of Women Voters.  

In line with her family’s pioneering interest in the cause of women’s modern education  
she served Boston University, her alma mater, as a trustee since 1907.  

Through long years her interest grew more sensitive humankind and her love for the  
countless subject people.  In that expansion of heart, herself remembering the nineties  
the ‘90s, she became a lifelong champion of small people.  Among her interests and  
celebrations was the priceless friendship that she has bestowed upon the individual  
she struggling with a public undercondition and neglected nation to bring into English  
verse “Armenian Poems: Rendered into English from Armenian” and two collections of  
the “Songs of Russia.”  

The atrocious oppressions of the Czar’s government led her to active work with  
the American friends of Russian Freedom.  Her warm cooperation and friendship  
with Mme. Catherine Breshkovsky extended over many years and included the editing  
of her autobiography and letters.  

In the struggle itself she have equally enlisted her quick sympathies on countless  
occasions.  Repeatedly she has gone alone and without adequate help among the people  
of the oppressed to get the facts, give fair aid and other direct functions.  

Five recent works of this remarkable and richly endowed nature, to train her talents to  
serving to popularize and appreciation: the Hungarian village poems of “In the Land  
of Grief and Gladness” (Yiddish), “Songs of Russia”, the Hungarian Poems of Petofi  
and “Some Spanish-American Poets”, — the latter a very recent translation from which  
the peoples of the Spanish-American countries are opened to mutual knowledge.  Her work  
still continues.  

Thus when at the last moment was Miss Blackwell chosen last year to receive the annual  
award of the National Institute for Immortals, the history of the Woman’s Hour and that  
of which bound her influence to the public weal.  Now at seventy-seven years of age she  
not only has contributed inestimable service as a great pioneer but has been for very many  
real and bestower of unfailing personal help to all those who have sought her wisdom and  
aid in the quest of freedom wherever and in whatever form it calls to her for help.
