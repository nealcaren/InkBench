Senator Attacks County  
Administration.  

BY PARKER BROWN.  

Both United States Senator Deneen  
and Representative Ruth Hanna Mc-  
Cormick, who has announced that she  
is a candidate to succeed him, last  
night sounded the opening guns of  
their campaign in a north side mass  
meeting of more than 2,000 persons,  
held in the Turner hall at Diversey  
parkway and Lincoln avenue.  

Senator Deneen addressed more than  
an hour to a recital of the record of  
the present Republican administra-  
tion, and the “Thompson-Nash-Ken-  
naer ring,” as he called it. Mrs. Mc-  
Cormick, who followed him, reiter-  
ated the charges she made in her  
recent letter announcing her candi-  
dacy, and declared that the issue is  
one of good or bad government and  
not one of personalities.  

Denounces County Rule.  

Since the election of 1927, said  
Senator Deneen, the “ring” has been  
in complete control of the county  
administration. It has imposed an  
“intolerable tax burden”; it has  
conducted the affairs of the county  
without regard to business princi-  
ples and without regard to the pub-  
lic welfare.  

“During this time,” he continued,  
“city and county government have  
been used as a political machine and  
not as an agency of public service.  
The domination of the ‘ring’ over  
the county has been complete. It  
has selected the candidates; it has  
dictated the policies; it has per-  
mitted the extravagance and incom-  
petence which have produced the  
existing intolerable conditions.”  

Penalty of Bankruptcy.  

The responsibility, the Senator  
said, “rests wholly upon this gang,  
which has betrayed the confidence  
of the people.”  

He declared that if such condi-  
tions continued “the inevitable pen-  
alty will be bankruptcy and finan-  
cial ruin.”  

“Unless a change is made,” he  
said, “the people of Cook county  
will be compelled to pay more and  
more taxes for less and less service.  
The worst feature of the situation  
is that public funds have been wast-  
ed and squandered while the mor-  
al and physical welfare of our peo-  
ple has been neglected.”  

Tells of Budget Figures.  

The Senator cited a series of fig-  
ures to show that despite a tremen-  
dous increase in taxes, the county’s  
debt has mounted steadily. He  
gave as an example the bureau of  
public welfare, which, he said, cost  
$2,000,000 more in 1928 than in 1924,  
although it rendered less service.  

“The county agents’ office,” he  
declared, “was operated in 1928 at  
a cost of $1,400,000, compared with  
$425,000 in 1924.”  

He told of the increase in high tax-  
es and in the bonded debt of the  
county, and said that the tax levy  
in the past was thirty per cent less  
than now.  

“Yet,” he protested, “conditions  
of community health and social  
welfare have deteriorated. People  
are being turned away from the  
hospitals because there is no room  
for them. Children are being de-  
nied proper care and attention be-  
cause there is not money enough to  
provide for them. While this has  
been going on, the ‘ring’ has been  
wasting and squandering the pub-  
lic’s money.”  

He asserted that “the greatest  
issue in this campaign is the ques-  
tion of public service. Our oppo-  
nents have betrayed the trust re-  
posed in them by the people.”  

Sounds Campaign Plea.  

Senator Deneen then outlined the  
Republican campaign on the cen-  
tral issues, and asserted that the  
only hope of the people of Cook  
county for real relief “lies in the  
election of a Republican ticket that  
is pledged to give to the people  
efficiency, honesty and economy in  
government.”  

Mrs. McCormick, who was intro-  
duced as “the next United States  
senator from Illinois,” opened her  
speech with a tribute to Senator  
Deneen.  

“I am not in this campaign to op-  
pose Senator Deneen,” she said. “I  
am in it to succeed him.”  

She reviewed the record of the Re-  
publican administration at Wash-  
ington, and declared that the “re-  
sponsibility for the present depres-  
sion rests squarely upon the shoul-  
ders of the Democratic administra-  
tion in the city and county of  
Cook.”  

HEALTH QUEEN  

[photo of young woman]  

Virginia McNeal of Maywood, Ill.,  
today was selected as healthful girl  
of Cook county.  

(Associated Press Photo.)  

“I am going to withdraw from the  
race in the Eighth district,” she  
said. “I am going to devote all my  
time and strength to this senatorial  
campaign.”  

She said that when she went into  
Congress she did so with the idea  
of rendering the best service that  
she could to the country at large.  
“After a study of the situation,” she  
continued, “I came to the conclu-  
sion that the greatest service I could  
render the country at this time is  
to take part in the fight that is be-  
ing waged in Illinois.”  

Urges Service to Nation.  

“What is my slogan? Easy.  
‘Service to the nation,’ ” she de-  
clared. “The call for this kind of  
service is never greater than today.  

“On every hand we find citizens  
eager and ready for the call of  
duty. The business men and wom-  
en of Chicago are anxious to have  
an opportunity to show their devo-  
tion to the country. They must be  
given that opportunity.”  

Mrs. McCormick asserted that  
there is a “new order in politics.”  

“The day of political dictatorship  
is gone,” she said. “The people want  
to govern themselves. They will not  
submit to domination by a politi-  
cal boss or by a political ring.”  

She praised President Hoover and  
his administration, and declared  
that the people of Illinois must give  
him “a Republican congress to sup-  
port him.”  

Going to Washington.  

Mrs. McCormick said that she in-  
tended to go to Washington to at-  
tend the opening of the next ses-  
sion of Congress, due to convene  
tomorrow, and would then return  
to Chicago to resume her campaign.  

She left last night for Washing-  
ton with Senator Deneen. While in  
Washington, she said, she would  
confer with other members of the  
Illinois delegation relative to the  
campaign.  

Organization on April 5.  

Preliminary organization of the  
campaign was effected at a meet-  
ing at which O. P. Meining, of  
A. W. Brown, vice chairman of the  
Republican state central committee,  
announced that committees will be  
called into session next Friday af-  
ternoon to tackle the task of reorganiza-  
tion in Cook county. These meet-  
ings are to be held in the Sherman  
hotel. Former State’s Attorney  
Robert E. Crowe heads the cam-  
paign in Cook county.  

Among the speakers at a fellows’  
dinner at the Morrison hotel last  
night were Martin Kennelly, form-  
er president of the Illinois Manu-  
facturers’ association; Thomas Gal-  
lagher, former state senator; Wil-  
liam H. Weber, former state sen-  
ator; Mrs. Grace Wilbur Trout, for-  
mer president of the Illinois Equal  
Suffrage association; Mrs. Ella  
Flagg Young, former superintend-  
ent of schools; Mrs. Katherine Han-  
nan, and Mrs. Arthur G. Logan.  


WIFE DIVORCES  
MORSE IN RENO;  
HE LOSES PLEA  

Reno, Nev., Dec. 9.—(Special.)—  
Ernest K. Morse, divorced by Mrs.  
Janet Kirkland Morse in the Chan-  
cery division of the district court  
here today, was refused the right  
to reopen the evidence in the case.  

Morse, who arrived here after his  
wife’s divorce action was started,  
charged that some of the evidence  
she produced in court was “fraudu-  
lent.”  

Mrs. Morse was allowed $75 month-  
ly alimony for one year and $150 as  
attorney’s fees.  

Morse was awarded $1,810 in per-  
sonal property and Mrs. Morse was  
given their residence in Chicago,  
valued at $5,000.  

Mrs. Morse charged cruel treat-  
ment. Morse denied the charge and  
alleged that his wife was guilty of  
infidelity.  

Mrs. Morse formerly lived in the  
north shore district of Chicago.  
Morse is an investment broker.
