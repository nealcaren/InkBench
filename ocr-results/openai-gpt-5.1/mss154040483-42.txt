Third Assembly he promptly taken, the at-  
tack for the regular meeting of the Ad-  
visory Committee on Traffic in Women  
and Children was advanced by several

our American representative, Miss Grace  
Abbott of the Children’s Bureau, went on  
“unofficial observer.”  

The full report of her action of that  
Committee has just now been given out.  
This informs us that the fullest possible  
consideration was given to the draft reso-  
lution which, after much discussion, was  
finally adopted. In this discus-  
sion women and important points were  
raised and were finally crystallized into  
resolutions. One of these recommended  
that a questionnaire be sent to all the  
members of the League asking should  
they report that a system of regula-  
tion was in working and results, and  
for those that have abolished it for  
them to state how they were enabled to  
abolish do so. The other urged the  
use of women police in dealing with  
this problem.  

But, says the report, particular im-  
portance attaches to that resolution  
which embodies the proposal made by  
the “unofficial observer” from the  
United States. This was that the Coun-  
cil might, without delay, appoint ex-  
perts to make, in collaboration with  
the governments of the countries con-  
cerned, a comprehensive study of the  
conditions under which this traffic goes  
on, the methods practiced and the main  
ends between which prevail. Again,  
it is seen, the mighty weapon of publi-  
city is to be used. Light is to be  
turned upon this darkest spot in our so-  
cial organism. It is hardly credible  
that, when the full facts are laid out  
for the world’s consideration, govern-  
ments and people will not be stirred to more  
determined positive action against the  
abuses now held in abeyance.  

When these resolutions of the Advis-  
ory Committee have passed through the  
necessary technical stages, they will be  
referred to the various national legis-  
latures for ratification. Adequate pro-  
vision for their enforcement must then  
be passed. And when that is done, as  
Mr. Blaine, alternate from Australia,  
said at the time of the discussion in the  
Third Assembly, the greatest blow yet  
dealt at the traffic will have been  
struck.  


THE WOMAN CITIZEN  

Your Investments  
By ELEANOR KENE[?]  

TWO policies issued by life insurance  
companies which are very popular  
and which are more or less on the en-  
dowment order mentioned in our last  
article, are income bonds and retire-  
ment annuities.  

Many women—and men, too, of  
course—who have no one dependent on  
them and who wish to provide themselves  
with savings from which to receive an  
income in the future, find the purchase  
of income bonds one of the safest meth-  
ods of assuring this. The purchaser  
makes annual payments until a certain  
age, after which the life insurance com-  
pany pays her a fixed annual monthly  
[?] for the rest of her life. Should she  
die before reaching this age, the in-  
surance company usually arranges to  
turn over to her heirs whatever lesser  
amount of deposits which have been  
made. And upon the death of the in-  
sured, after having reached the stipu-  
lated age and received some of the in-  
come payments, but not the aggregate  
equal to the entire amount  
deposited as premiums, the difference  
is usually returned to the heirs.  

The cost of income bonds, purchased  
at thirty, depends upon the age at  
which payment of income begins and  
the age at which the income is desired.  
A policy bought at the age of thirty in  
which income begins at the age of fifty,  
and which furnishes an income of twen-  
ty-five hundred dollars annually, would  
require ten dollars weekly payments  
for twenty years. A policy which  
would begin income at sixty, and fur-  
nish ten dollars weekly beginning then  
for the rest of the insured’s life, would  
require ten dollars monthly beginning  
then for the rest of the insured’s life,  
would require ten dollars weekly for  
twenty years. If payment began at  
the age of thirty, she would pay an an-  
nual premium of about $341.41, and an  
equal amount for each additional ten  
dollars of income desired. Should she  
live out the terms of the policy and the  
entire amount of premiums would be  
about $82.36. Disregarding the fact  
that no interest is placed in the end  
of twenty or thirty years, the interest  
received by her at that period amounts  
to a little over 7 per cent. on the accu-  
mulated deposits in the latter case, and  
approximately 11½ per cent. in the  
former case. The profit to the insur-  
ance company, of course, lies in the  
chief interest on the money as it  
is annually during the twenty or  
thirty year periods is paid out, but  
goes into the company’s treasury. From  
a business man’s point of view, this  
makes the interest received after the  
beginning of income for women at the  
age of fifty or sixty represent only  
about one-half the interest rate indi-  
cated as accruing during the time capi-  
tal has had a chance to increase 50 per cent.  
to 100 per cent. The compensation for  
this lower rate lies in the protection  
against making a bad investment  
in the guarantee of systematic sav-  
ing. The insurance and bond agents op-  
erate on the same principle and is practi-  
cally the same thing, though differently  
named. The premium on $1000 is  
$1105 a year. This would provide for  
a woman buying a retirement annuity  
of $15.30 a month for life beginning  
at the age of fifty, and of $20.71 at  
the age of sixty.
