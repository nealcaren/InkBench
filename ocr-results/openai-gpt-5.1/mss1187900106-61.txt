JOHNSON'S UNIV. CYC. VOL. III  

Liberia, a republic on the western coast of Africa, founded in 1820 by the American Colonization Society (see COLONIZATION SOCIETY), and established as an independent state in 1847; it lies between 4° 20' and 7° 30' N. lat., and stretches along the Atlantic from the Sherbro river on the N. W. to the Pedro river on the S. E., a distance of about 500 miles, extending inland from the coast from 10 to 40 miles. Its area, which has steadily increased by purchase from the native tribes, was estimated in 1854 at 10,000 square miles. The shore is elevated and rocky in the N. W., but otherwise low, generally sandy or gravelly, seldom more than 10 ft. high. In the interior the country rises, swelling into more rugged hills and lofty mountain-ranges traversed by fine valleys. Many streams flow down to the coast, but none of them is navigable for any great distance from its mouth; the St. Paul, the St. John, the Junk, and the Cavalla are the most important. The climate is hot and very moist, the year being divided into a rainy and a dry season. The rainy season lasts from May to November, during which the rain sometimes falls in torrents for several days without intermission. The mean temperature at Monrovia is about 79° F.; in the hottest months the thermometer rarely rises above 90° in the shade, and in the coolest it seldom falls below 70°. The climate is very unhealthy to Europeans, but is comparatively favorable to the negro race. The soil is generally fertile, yielding, with little labor, rice, cassava, yams, sweet potatoes, Indian corn, coffee, sugar-cane, cotton, indigo, arrow-root, ginger, tobacco, and many tropical fruits. The forests contain a great variety of valuable timber, india-rubber, palm, and other useful trees. Iron ore is abundant, and gold is said to exist in the interior, but no systematic mining has yet been undertaken. The chief exports are palm-oil, camwood, ivory, sugar, coffee, rice, and a small quantity of gold-dust; the imports are cotton and woollen goods, hardware, tobacco, spirits, etc. The foreign trade is carried on mainly by English and American merchants; the total value of exports and imports is estimated at from $1,000,000 to $1,500,000 annually. Coffee-culture has of late years been largely extended in the interior, and bids fair to become a source of great wealth.  

The population consists of two classes, the Americo-Liberians, or descendants of emancipated slaves from the United States and the West Indies, and the aboriginal tribes. The former, who number about 15,000, are chiefly settled along the coast, and are engaged in agriculture, trade, and the learned professions. They have adopted the dress, habits, and institutions of the United States, and are generally intelligent and industrious. The native population, estimated at from 600,000 to 800,000, belongs to various negro tribes, differing in language and customs, and in different stages of advancement. Along the coast they are generally peaceable, and many of them have adopted Christianity and civilized habits; in the interior they are still mostly heathen, though Mohammedanism is spreading among some of the tribes. Missionary operations have been carried on since the foundation of the colony by various religious bodies in the United States and Europe. The American Episcopal, Methodist, Baptist, and Presbyterian churches have missions, schools, and churches in different parts of the republic, and have made considerable progress in diffusing Christianity and education.  

The government of Liberia is republican, modelled in great part after that of the United States. The constitution was adopted in 1847, and provides for a president, elected for two years, a vice-president, and a legislature consisting of a senate and house of representatives. The president must be a citizen of Liberia by birth, or must have resided in the republic for at least three years, and must be possessed of real estate. The legislature meets annually at Monrovia. The judiciary consists of a supreme court, whose judges are appointed by the president with the consent of the senate, and of inferior courts. Trial by jury is established. The suffrage is confined to civilized male citizens of African descent, 21 years of age and upward, who possess real estate. The militia embraces all able-bodied male citizens between the ages of 16 and 50. The revenue is derived chiefly from customs duties, which range from 10 to 25 per cent. ad valorem; the expenditure is mainly for the support of the civil government, the army and navy, and public schools. The public debt is small. Education is encouraged by the government; there are primary schools in most of the settlements, and several higher schools and academies. Liberia College, at Monrovia, founded in 1851, and opened in 1862, affords instruction in the higher branches of learning.  

The history of Liberia begins with the efforts of the American Colonization Society, organized at Washington in 1816, to establish a colony on the west coast of Africa, to which free people of color from the United States might emigrate. In 1820 the first company of emigrants, about 80 in number, sailed from New York, and after various difficulties a permanent settlement was made at Cape Mesurado, now Monrovia, in 1822. Other settlements were gradually formed along the coast, and in 1839 they were united under the name of the Commonwealth of Liberia, with a governor appointed by the Colonization Society. In 1847 the people declared themselves independent, and adopted a republican constitution. The independence of Liberia was soon after acknowledged by Great Britain, France, and other European powers, and in 1862 by the United States. Since that time the territory of the republic has been enlarged by treaties with the native chiefs and by purchase from foreign powers. The progress of the country has been retarded by want of capital and of an industrious laboring class, by the hostility of some of the native tribes, and by the competition of foreign traders; but the stability of its government and the gradual spread of civilization and Christianity among the natives give promise of a more prosperous future.
