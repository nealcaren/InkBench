This No. is the sequel to No. 1, the preparation for No. 3. The directions here given take for granted that those on the cover of No. 1 have been studied and followed. In this No., the letters formed from the first four principles are reviewed, and the stem-letters, b, [?], f, h, k, l, p, q, are introduced.  

The Contractions used are as follows: — m. l., Main Line; l. c., Connecting Line; m. s., Main Line Slope; s. c., Connecting Line Slope; m[?]., Modified; pl., Parallel; Pr., Principle; El., Element, etc.  

The Diagram on the cover shows the Elements, Principles, and Scale of Lengths. The Diagram above the copy, the part made at each count, the Principle illustrated, etc., of each copy is to be placed in the lines.  

SUGGESTIONS. 1. The only possible way of teaching Penmanship successfully in a school, is, to have all the scholars of a class write in the same line in the same book at the same time.  

2. The order of instruction is KNOWLEDGE, EXECUTION, CRITICISM.  

First, KNOWLEDGE. Explain the copy carefully, and have it analyzed into its Principles and into its Elements. Teach the nature, peculiarity, scope, beginning and ending of each element; notice the connections and the parallelism of lines; observe the relation between the letters; call attention to similitude and variety of parts and lines.  

Second, EXECUTION. Let them draw the horizontal and vertical lines. Then have one line in the column written, directing the pupils to be very careful as to position, holding of pen, rests, and movements, which are explained in the Manual and on cover of No. 1, that they do not allow the pen to be raised from the paper until the line is written except in p. Be sure the wrist does not touch; it should be high enough from the desk to allow a holder to be passed under it from the right side.  

Third, CRITICISM. Criticise the line written, by asking questions on each particular given as knowledge, which the class answer by raising their hands. The errors lie, of course, on each side of the truth. Thus, if the line is a curve, it may be curved the wrong way, or too much or too little; if sloped, one line too much or too little; if too narrow or too broad, or too narrow. Select one or two of the most prevalent errors, discovered by the answers and by your own observation, for correction; direct especial attention to them, and have one more line written that they may be corrected. Then criticise these same faults. If the class have succeeded in correcting them, call their attention to another fault in the execution of each line, and direct attention to the correction of that fault until the whole is perfected.  

GENERAL RULES. 1. Begin and end the p. in the 2d s. Every principle touches both the head and base lines. 2. The Main Lines are the straight lines, and the sides of the oval written downward; the ovals, the second upstroke of l and t, and the second round of w. See Manual, Chap. III. The Connecting Lines are the parts of the letters with upward turns. The Turns unite main to connecting lines. Sometimes the union is perfect and sometimes it is a point; this is termed the point. The line formed between two letters by their connecting lines running into one another at the middle of the space is termed a Combining Line. The odd numbers are used for the upstrokes; the even, for the down. 5. There are six general kinds of connecting lines, which occur in the principles. There are six Principles in the small letters; from the last six, the combination of all the principles and all their letters are made. 6. The red lines mark the columns, each of which is divided into three oblongs or boxes. Write down the column always. 7. The m. s. is 5[?]° from base-line.  

N. B. Teachers will find our Manual of Penmanship an able compendium of our art of teaching writing. The Blackboard Tablets are invaluable for presenting the Elements, Principles, and Capitals, of large size and perfect form. The Oblique Lines are a great help to the scholars in acquiring correct slope.
