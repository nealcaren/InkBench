JOHNSON'S UNIV. CYC. VOL. III

Liberia, a republic on the western coast of Africa, founded in 1820 by the American Colonization Society (see COLONIZATION SOC.), and established as an independent state in 1847. The republic stretches along the Atlantic from the Sherboro River on the N. W. to the Pdro River on the S. E., a distance of about 350 miles; and extends inland from the coast from 10 to 40 miles. Its area, which is steadily increased by purchases from the native tribes, was estimated at 43,000 square miles. The shore is elevated and rocky in the S. E., but otherwise low, generally sandy or gravelly, seldom marshy. The interior consists of elevated plateaus, interrupted by hills and lofty mountain ranges traversed by fine valleys. Many streams flow to the ocean, but none of them is navigable for more than 20 miles from the mouth; the most important are the St. Paul, navigable for 18 miles, the Junk, the Cestos, and the Farmington; the St. John, the Junk, and the Cape Mount River. The climate is thoroughly tropical. Of the two seasons, the dry from November to June, and the wet from June to October. In the dry season the average heat is 84° F., the thermometer sometimes rising to 90°. In the wet season the average heat is 76°, the thermometer never falling below 60°. To the white man the climate is decidedly unhealthy, though less so than formerly from malaria; and even the negro, when born and raised in another climate, suffers on his arrival here from what is called African fever. The natives, on the contrary, are robust, healthy, and long-lived. The soil is generally very fertile, and the country well watered, especially in the valley of the St. Paul. Here the sugar-cane grows luxuriantly; one year's produce was estimated at 360,000 pounds. Coffee of excellent quality is cultivated with success in the interior. The cereals, including rice and cassava, the vegetables, cabbages, peas, beans, tomatoes, cucumbers, etc.; and the fruits, lemons, oranges, guavas, tamarinds, pine-apples, cocoa-nuts, and plantains are extensively raised. The forests contain teak, mahogany, rosewood, ebony, poplar, several kinds of gum trees, dyewood, musk shrubs, and different varieties of useful palms, among which is the nut-bearing palm, from which palm oil is made. The ivory trade is carried on to some extent, though the supplies, etc., are now nearly exterminated. Of minerals, iron abounds, and copper is said to occur in the interior. The population, not yet numbered, according to the largest estimates, about 1,668,000, of whom about 18,000 are Americo-Liberians, and the remainder belong to different tribes; the Veys, mostly Mohammedans, among whom the Protestant Episcopal Church of the U.S. has established a mission; the Mandingoes, entirely pagans; the Bassas, among whom the American Baptist missionaries established a mission in 1835; the Kroos, mostly idolaters; the Mandings, the most gifted of the tribes under the jurisdiction of the republic; and others. The people have schools, and are progressing in all branches of civilization. Industrial processes and manufactures have been started under a liberal government treaty existing between the republic and the U.S., Great Britain, Belgium, and Hamburg. Palm oil, sugar, cotton, coffee, arrow-root, etc. are exported; cotton goods, cutlery, powder, and tobacco are imported. The country is divided into four counties, Montserrado, Grand Cape Mount, and Maryland. The capital, Monrovia, is situated on Cape Mesurado, and has about 3,600 inhabitants. Other settlements are Buchanan, Caldwell, Virginia, Edina, Greenville, Lexington, Buchanan, Millsburg, etc. The annual revenue is about $100,000, and is devoted to the home duties. A public debt of $300,000 was contracted in 1871. The constitution of the republic is modelled after that of the United States, but free men are equal. Elections take place by ballot, and every male citizen who possesses real estate has the right of suffrage. Foreigners can be admitted to citizenship, and none but citizens can hold real estate in the republic (a temporary provision). The legislature consists of a senate of four; the representatives for two. Each county sends two senators to the legislative assembly, and one representative for 1,000 inhabitants. The first president was Joseph Jenkins Roberts, who served four terms, from 1848 to 1856, and was re-elected in 1872; Edward James Roye was elected president in 1873; and H. R. W. Johnson was elected president May, 1883. The official language is the English. (See Stockwell, The Republic of Liberia; Hilliard, Life and Labor in Western Africa; a History of its Early Settlement, New York, 1868; and Li- beria; the Americo-African Republic, New York, 1883.)
