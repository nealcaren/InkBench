"The Governor means right," said Mrs. Howe. "He means that, in view of the legitimacy and injustice of the inhibition necessitated by the law, the women may very well have obtained from voting." 

"Do you agree with the Governor in his implication that woman suffrage may prove to be an unfortunate experiment?  The point that I should make," replied Mrs. Howe, "is, who has the right to determine whether it is or is not?  We do not admit that the right to make the decision. There are some who do not admit that universal male suffrage is good, but such are not allowed to limit the suffrage. Suffrage does not belong to men to give or to take away. The masculine form of law men must give it to us.  I do not allow that the obligation to give it, though it shall not be an 'unkindness,' shall be an unconstitutional experiment."

"How do you view the Governor's proposal to have women decide whether they were enfranchised by the women themselves?"

"It is a very doubtful matter to speak of, but I will say that I think that a matter of such evident and positive right should be submitted to the chance of a popular vote."

"What would the effect be, do you think, of such a vote?"

"Just now you see that, if we settle the question in that manner, it turns out in our favor, we can do nothing more in the same way. "How would the majority of the women vote on this question were it so submitted to them?"

"I should feel very uncertain that, about that. Women of the great leisure class would go against it, and, while I do not wish to do the poorer classes any injustice, I fear that a great many of them will be deceived. There are also a good many young women in easy circumstances who would fear to disappoint their husbands, lovers, and fathers.  It is nearly all women graduates of colleges, and all professional women who vote 'yes.'"

"Do you view the Governor's plan of postponing the office voting?"

"The experiment of voting for school officers places developed nothing which rendered it unpleasant or improper for a woman to go there and cast her ballot. In view of the falsification, women should vote as men do."

"What do you aim for particularly in the immediate future?"

"We should always strike for municipal suffrage, which thing we cannot for some.  We must never for a moment lose sight of all that we mean by suffrage. We must advance in every practical manner, at every opportunity."

"What, in your judgment is the outlook?"

"The rapid rise in the culture of women and particularly the great increases of the study of sociological and economic questions, are an carrying society in the direction of women's enfranchisement, in spite of what individuals may do or think."
