An undaunted devotion to the cause of woman suffrage links the name of Alice Stone Blackwell, counselor of the Boston League of Women Voters, with that of her famous mother, Lucy Stone. Her father, Henry B. Blackwell, really devoted his life to obtaining equal rights for women. One of Miss Blackwell's aunts, Elizabeth B. Blackwell, was the first woman to take a medical degree, and another, Antoinette Brown Blackwell, was ordained a minister. For many years she appeared annually before the committee of the Massachusetts Legislature to answer the anti-suffrage arguments, and a distinguished lawyer once said he considered her rebuttals the ablest presentations of controversial matter he had ever heard.  It was also Miss Blackwell who brought together the two parties of the suffrage movement, whose estrangement weakened their effectiveness, welding them in 1890 into the National American Woman Suffrage Association, of which she was secretary for nearly 20 years.  For years she has been a trustee of Boston University and is a member of Phi Beta Kappa. Close to her heart is the cause of the Armenians. She has rendered into English a volume of Armenian poems, as well as one of Russian poems a volume of Yiddish verse, and one, still unpublished, of Mexican and South American poetry.
