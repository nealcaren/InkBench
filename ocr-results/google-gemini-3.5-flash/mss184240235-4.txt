My dear Miss Dickinson
        That you should last evening have
experienced any inconvenience whatever
has been with me a cause of much regret, yet
Can I not admit that the responsibility there-
for rests at all on me. My position in re-
gard to succession to the presidential chair
is as well known as that of almost any
other person in the city, and I cannot but
regard it as having been a great mistake
on the part of your friends that they should
have urged upon me in any manner to
sanction a lecture the whole drift of
which was to be in direct opposition to
my own published views. -
        In the very brief conversation which
preceded my refusal you expressed surprise
that I should have known nothing of a lecture
that you had delivered at Cooper Institute
six months before and since more than
once repeated. - The fact, nevertheless, was
so, as I had then, and have now, no
recollection of having ever heard or read a single
line of any lecture at all resembling that
