Freedmen's Bureau.

The work of the Freedmen's Bureau, for children, gives
promise of developing into a system of schools for their
education.  The evening schools already established, and those supplied homes.

General Rufus Saxton, as is known, being most of the war
in charge of the Department of the South, became familiar
with the wants of the colored people.  Several of
the schools which he found in existence under his direction, during the war,
were in North & South Carolina, Florida & Georgia.  Himself
active in the work, he gave his special attention to
the establishment of schools for the colored people in those
sections since the close of the war.  A few weeks ago, he was
appointed Assistant Commissioner for South Carolina, Georgia & Florida, & has
since been actively engaged in the work of organizing
schools with a view to the permanent education of the
Freedmen in those States.

The plan which he has adopted is to induce the Freedmen
themselves to contribute to the erection of school buildings
for their own use.  To this end, with the assistance of
the officers who have been detailed for the purpose, he has
succeeded in raising large sums of money for the purpose,
amounting in some instances to over a thousand dollars,
from the earnings of the Freedmen themselves.  These
contributions have been made, in some cases, with so much difficulty that
the buildings erected have been of the most humble character,
being mere huts of palmetto leaves or rough boards.  In
other cases, more money has been raised, and a better
school house has been built.  In one instance, in Georgia, a
school house has been erected at a cost of over $2000,
by the contributions of the Freedmen alone.

General Saxton has also induced the white people of the
South to contribute to the erection of school houses for
the Freedmen.  In several instances, white citizens have
subscribed liberally to this object, and in one case,
in Georgia, a gentleman has given a lot of ground for the
purpose, and has himself built a school house at his own
expense, at a cost of over $1000.

The General has also succeeded in inducing several benevolent
societies in the North to contribute to the erection of
school houses in the South.  The American Missionary Association
has built several school houses in South Carolina, and
the New England Freedmen's Aid Society has built others
in Georgia.

By these combined efforts, a large number of school houses
have been erected in the three States under General Saxton's
charge, and the work is still going on.  The number of
schools established is already considerable, and is rapidly
increasing.  The attendance of pupils is large, and the
schools are in a flourishing condition.

This system of school houses, built by the contributions
of the Freedmen themselves, and of the white people of the
South, and of benevolent societies in the North, is one
of the most hopeful signs of the times.  It shows that
the Freedmen are willing to contribute of their scanty
means for the education of their children, and that the
white people of the South are beginning to see the
importance of educating the colored race.  It is a system
which, if persevered in, will result in the establishment
of a permanent system of schools for the education of the
Freedmen, which will be a lasting benefit to the South,
and to the whole country.

The work of the Freedmen's Bureau in this direction is
one which deserves the hearty support of the people of the
North.  It is a work which is to be done by the people
themselves, and not by the Government.  The Bureau only
assists in organizing the work, and in securing the
co-operation of all interested parties.  The school houses
once built, the schools are to be sustained by the people
themselves, by contributions from the Freedmen, from the
white people, and from benevolent societies.  The Government
does not undertake to build the school houses, nor to
support the schools after they are established.

This system of self-supporting schools is one which is
well adapted to the wants of the Freedmen.  It gives them
an interest in the schools, and makes them feel that they
are their own institutions, built by their own contributions,
and to be sustained by their own efforts.  It also
secures the co-operation of the white people of the South,
who see that the schools are not to be supported by the
Government, but by the people themselves.

The success of this system in South Carolina, Georgia and
Florida, under the direction of General Saxton, is a
sufficient guarantee that it can be extended to other
States of the South, with equal success.  It is a system
which commends itself to the judgment of all who desire
the permanent improvement of the colored race, and the
peace and prosperity of the South.
