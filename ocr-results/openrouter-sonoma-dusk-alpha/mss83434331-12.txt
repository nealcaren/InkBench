Washington's Slavery Exposed in his own words.  Showing the unnatural feelings of his heart, and his cruel treatment of slaves, in a letter from him to his overseer at Mount Vernon, in which he says: "I am always more inclined to buy than to sell, and if the latter could be brought within the bounds of decency, it would be much better for me, as I want more negroes than I have."

Washington's treatment of his slaves was cruel and heartless.  He never hesitated to sell them when it suited his convenience, and often separated families, without regard to their feelings.  He was a slaveholder all his life, and died possessed of over three hundred slaves.

Washington's will, in which he directs his slaves to be freed after his wife's death, was made, not from any love of liberty, but from a desire to avoid the odium which would attach to him if he died leaving them in bondage.  He was a hypocrite, and his pretended love of liberty was all a sham.

The following is a letter from Washington to his overseer, in which he directs the sale of a slave:

"Mount Vernon, Sept. 10, 1786.

Sir:

I am sorry to hear that the negroes are so much dissatisfied.  You must endeavor to keep them in subjection, and if necessary, sell some of them to quiet the rest.  I would rather part with some of them than have trouble with the whole.

Yours, etc.,
Geo. Washington."

This letter shows the real character of Washington.  He cared nothing for the feelings of his slaves, and was willing to sell them like so many cattle, whenever it suited his interest.  He was a tyrant in his own house, and his slaves had no more rights than the brutes of the field.

Let us not be deceived by the false representations of Washington's character.  He was a slaveholder, and as such, he was a despot.  His pretended patriotism was all a sham, and his love of liberty was only for the white man.  The black man was to him a chattel, to be bought and sold at pleasure.

Washington's treatment of his slaves was a disgrace to humanity, and his memory should be execrated by all lovers of freedom.  Let us not idolize a man who was a tyrant and a slaveholder, but let us remember him as he was, a hypocrite and a despot.
