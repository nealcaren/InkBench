Exposition of the Fragment sent

to the friends of the late Thomas Garrett by
his daughter Mary T. Garrett

After the death of her father, which occurred on the 25th of January 1871, his daughter Mary T. Garrett found among his papers the following fragment of a letter, which had been written to him by a lady in the North, and which she thinks may be of interest to his friends. It is as follows.

"In the midst of the gloom which surrounds me, I have not been without gleams of light. I have been permitted to witness the operation of the law of life and love in the case of a fugitive slave. The slave, who was about 30 years of age, had been sent by his master from Georgia to Richmond, Va., to sell goods, and while there he made his escape. He was pursued, captured, and put in jail, from which he was rescued by a colored man, who assisted him to get on board a vessel bound to Philadelphia. The vessel was pursued by the steamer that had been sent in search of him, but owing to the darkness of the night, and the skill of the captain, he eluded pursuit, and arrived safely in Philadelphia. From thence he was forwarded to my residence, where he remained until he was provided with employment in the city. He is now a sober, industrious man, and is doing well. This is one of the many instances that might be adduced to show the beneficial effects of the operation of the law of love in the case of the fugitive slave."

This narrative, though brief, is full of interest, and shows the heroism and devotion of those who were engaged in the work of aiding the escape of slaves. It also illustrates the dangers and difficulties that attended the undertaking, and the gratitude and affection which the fugitives felt towards their deliverers.

The following lines, written by John G. Whittier, on the death of Thomas Garrett, are deemed appropriate to this occasion:

"Thou hast gone from us, but thy work remains;
The slave is free, and lifts his chains
No more in sorrow or in pain;
The oppressed of earth, in every land,
Shall bless the name of Garrett, and
The good he did shall live for aye,
A monument of love's pure ray."

Mary T. Garrett.
