Gordon Parks

Born in Fort Scott, Kansas, November 30, 1912, Gordon Parks received his home education from a family of 15 children. In 1942, he was the first Black staff photographer for Standard Oil Company, where he worked on a documentary project. In 1948, he became the first African American photographer for Life magazine, where he remained for 20 years, producing major photo essays on such themes as civil rights, poverty, and Black nationalism. Parks was also a staff writer for Life, where he wrote the novel The Learning Tree, published in 1963. The book was made into a film, which Parks wrote and directed. In 1969, Parks was the first African American to direct a major motion picture, The Learning Tree. He went on to direct such films as Shaft (1971) and Shaft's Big Score (1972). Parks also wrote several volumes of poetry, including A Poet and His Camera (1968), and in 1971 published his autobiography, A Choice of Weapons. In 1987, he received the NAACP's Spingarn Medal. Parks has three children, including filmmaker St. Clair Bourne, and one grandchild.
