VI. Reply to Freeman's Bureau

"I. M. will pay his respects"

To the President of the United States:

Sir: We, the undersigned, members of the Board of Police Commissioners of the city of Memphis, respectfully represent that the laws of the State of Tennessee have been violated by the action of the Freedmen's Bureau in this city, and that said violations have been sanctioned by the military authorities. The President has referred the matter to the Attorney General, whereupon the Attorney General has referred it back to the President with the statement that the laws have been violated, but that he is without authority to act in the premises. This decision leaves the situation without remedy, unless the President shall interpose his authority to correct the evil. The following are the facts:

The Freedmen's Bureau was established by act of Congress for the protection of freedmen and refugees, and their interests. That object, we believe, has been accomplished, and the Bureau should now be abolished.

The course pursued by the present agent of the Freedmen's Bureau in this city has been such as to render it impossible for the civil authorities to enforce the laws, and the present state of things cannot continue without endangering the peace and safety of the community.

The Board of Police Commissioners have been unable to perform their duties, and the military authorities have sustained the agent of the Freedmen's Bureau in his usurpation of power.

We therefore respectfully urge the President to abolish the Freedmen's Bureau in this city, and to direct the military authorities to sustain the civil authorities in the enforcement of the laws.

Very respectfully,
your obedient servants,
Board of Police Commissioners

Very respectfully,
your obedient servant,

My Dear Sir:

I enclose you a copy of the correspondence between the President and the Attorney General in relation to the action of the Freedmen's Bureau in Memphis. I think the course pursued by the agent of the Freedmen's Bureau in that city has been such as to render it impossible for the civil authorities to enforce the laws, and that the Bureau should be abolished.

Your friend,
G. B. McClellan
