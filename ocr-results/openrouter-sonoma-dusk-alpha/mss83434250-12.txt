PREFACE.

Slavery is the holding of any person as property, or the denial to him, by law or custom, of any civil or political right.  Affronts where they exist, and so long as they do exist, of course cannot be remedied by the laws of the nation, nor even by the constitution of the United States, except in so far as these can be brought to bear upon the subject.  Yet, in so much as the laws of the States, and of the nation, so far from remedying these evils, have been made the instruments of perpetuating them, it is right and proper that they should be changed.  It is no less the right of the government than of individuals to amend the laws, when they are found to be unjust, or to withhold their aid from the execution of such as are so.  And, in a country like ours, where the government is, or ought to be, the representative of the people, and amenable to their control, it is the duty of the government to do this.  Sections IV., A, C, in which the efforts of some of the States to deprive free persons of color of every civil right, are particularly referred to, show to what extent the people of those States have carried their hostility to the colored race; and Section V. shows how this hostility has been extended to the government and laws of the United States.  No longer citizens of the United States, nor in any sense subjects of its government, nor enjoying longer its protection, it is important that they should be relieved from the operation of those laws which were intended for their benefit, and which, in a matter essentially domestic, operate only to their injury.  Unless this is done, the national government will be deprived of a powerful means of protecting its citizens in the enjoyment of their domestic rights; and the States will be encouraged to proceed to the utmost lengths in their oppression of their colored inhabitants.  Upon this, the national government has now conferred a power which it did not before possess; and the States, in the exercise of this power, have imposed upon the overnment such burdens as are unnecessary, and incompatible with the ends for which it was instituted.  Unless its own existence is maintained; [with a deadly struggle] it is the duty of the government to relieve itself from these burdens.  It is the duty of the government to protect its own existence; and, in so doing, to protect the rights of the States, and of the people of the States.  It is the duty of the government to protect the States in the exercise of their reserved rights; and, in so doing, to protect the people of the States in the enjoyment of those rights.  It is the duty of the government to protect the people of the States in the enjoyment of their domestic rights; and, in so doing, to protect the States in the exercise of their reserved rights.  It is the duty of the government to protect the States in the exercise of their reserved rights; and, in so doing, to protect the people of the States in the enjoyment of their domestic rights.  It is the duty of the government to protect the people of the States in the enjoyment of their domestic rights; and, in so doing, to protect the States in the exercise of their reserved rights.
