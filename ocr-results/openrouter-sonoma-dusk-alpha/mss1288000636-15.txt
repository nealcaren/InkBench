### Main Article: The Cruise of the S.S. Vestris

#### Arrival of the Vestris
The S.S. Vestris, the ill-fated liner which sank off the Virginia Capes on November 12, 1928, with a loss of more than 100 lives, arrived in New York yesterday morning after a voyage from Buenos Aires that was marked by no unusual incident.  She carried 198 passengers and a crew of 190, and was under the command of Capt. W.H. Stanley, who took over after the death of Capt. Frederick P. Brown, who was in charge when the Vestris went down.

The Vestris, which had been rebuilt at a cost of more than $1,000,000, presented a trim appearance as she docked at Pier 60, North River.  Her hull, which had been ripped open in the disaster, had been repaired and strengthened, and her superstructure, which had been damaged by fire after she was salvaged, had been restored.  She was greeted by a crowd of several hundred persons, including relatives of passengers and members of the crew, who had been on board during her last voyage.

#### Survivors' Stories
Among the passengers who came ashore was Mrs. Mary Keys, of Richmond, Va., who was returning from a visit to her son in Argentina.  Mrs. Keys, who was 68 years old, said that the voyage had been pleasant and that she had not experienced any seasickness.  "The ship is much improved since her last trip," she said.  "The new captain is very efficient, and the crew is well trained.  I had no fears whatever."

Another passenger, John H. Smith, a businessman from Philadelphia, told reporters that the Vestris had encountered heavy weather off the Brazilian coast, but that the ship had behaved excellently.  "We had a fine time," he said.  "The food was good, the service was excellent, and the entertainment was first-class.  I would not hesitate to sail on her again."

#### Crew Members Speak
Members of the crew were more reticent in their comments.  One of the stewards, who declined to give his name, said: "We've got a good ship now, and we're glad to be back in service.  But we'll never forget what happened last time."  A member of the engine room force added: "The engines are in perfect condition, and the boilers are new.  There's no reason why she shouldn't make many more voyages."

Capt. Stanley, in a brief statement, said that the Vestris was in every respect seaworthy and that he was confident of her future.  "We have a picked crew," he said, "and every man knows his job.  The lessons of the past have been learned, and we are prepared for any emergency."

#### The Vestris Disaster Recalled
The sinking of the Vestris, one of the worst maritime disasters in recent years, occurred when the ship, bound from New York to Rio de Janeiro, encountered a gale off the Virginia Capes.  Heavy seas stove in her plates, and she began to list heavily.  Despite heroic efforts by the crew to save her, she foundered, and many passengers and members of the crew lost their lives.  The disaster led to a thorough investigation by the government and to improvements in safety regulations for passenger ships.

The Vestris was raised by the Merritt-Chapman & Scott Corporation and taken to New York for repairs.  During the salvage operations, a fire broke out on board, but it was quickly extinguished.  After the repairs were completed, she underwent successful trials and was certified by the Bureau of Navigation as fit for sea.

#### Future Plans
The Vestris is scheduled to resume her regular service between New York and South American ports.  Her next voyage will be to Buenos Aires, sailing on December 15.  Already bookings are heavy, and the steamship company expects a full passenger list.  The tragedy of a year ago seems to have been forgotten, and the Vestris is once more a popular ship.

### Sidebar: This and That

#### Gold Book News
The Gold Book, the annual directory of the social register, will be issued next week.  It contains the names of more than 10,000 families in New York and other cities.  The book, which is edited by Louis H. Gold, is regarded as the authoritative guide to New York's aristocracy.

#### Building Trends
Construction activity in Manhattan continues at a high level.  During November, permits were issued for buildings costing more than $20,000,000.  The largest project is the new office building at 120 Broadway, which will have 50 stories and will cost $15,000,000.

#### Weather Notes
A cold wave is sweeping over the Middle West, with temperatures dropping to zero in parts of Illinois.  In New York, the mercury stood at 28 degrees at 8 A.M. today, and snow flurries are predicted for tonight.
