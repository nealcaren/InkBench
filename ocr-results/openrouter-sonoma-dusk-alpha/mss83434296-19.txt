Decr. No. L.  [Bost.]

You will at length have the pleasure to witness the
restoration of the South to its proper place in the
Union, & the submission of the authors of the late
rebellion to the authority of the government.  As
a citizen of the United States, I cannot but rejoice
at this result.  By it we are preserved from the
disgrace of having our name blotted from the list
of nations, & from the still greater calamity of
seeing our country dismembered, & the glorious
work of our fathers undone.

But for myself, I have no desire to see any of the
rebels punished, unless it be by the law, & in such
cases as my own.  In which it is my own, I have
no objection to their being sent to the Penitentiary
for life.  But I do not believe that any of them
deserve such punishment.  They were led into the
war by their leaders, & when they found they could
not succeed, they submitted.  The leaders should
be punished, but not the soldiers.  The soldiers
were as much the victims of the leaders as we were.

The President has done well in granting a general
amnesty, & I hope Congress will follow his example.
There is no use in keeping up the feeling of hatred
& revenge.  The war is over, & we should now
endeavor to heal the wounds it has made, & to
restore the country to its former prosperity.

I have no doubt that the South will soon recover
from the effects of the war, & that we shall see
the old Union restored, with all its former glory.

Decr. 18th 1865.
