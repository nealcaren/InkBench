Remarks of Hon. Wm. D. Kelly, of Pennsylvania,

Delivered in the House of Representatives, February 24, 1863.

Mr. Speaker, the motion now pending is one of the most important
brought before this House during the present session.  The
great danger, however, lies not in the motion, but in the
powerful influence which it will exert upon the
minds of those in Kentucky whose prejudices and
prepossessions have been so long nursed by the
slavery propagandists of that State.  That influence
may be salutary, or it may be otherwise.  The
question is, whether, when we have in our hands, as
we now have, the power to eradicate slavery, we
shall use it to extirpate this great cause of the
rebellion, or whether we shall so employ it as to
render the reestablishment of slavery in Virginia
and Kentucky possible.  The gentlemen, with whom
I have been associated in the effort to carry the
Constitution and the flag into the rebel States, have
been so anxious to secure the cooperation of loyal
men in all the States, that they have been willing to
go as far as possible to accomplish that object.
They have, therefore, consented that the proposition
should be so modified as to declare that Congress
will not interfere with slavery in the States where
it now exists.  In this, I think, they have gone too
far; but as they have done so for the purpose of
securing the cooperation of the loyal men of Kentucky,
I am not disposed to oppose it.  But I do not
think that we should go further.  By the delay which
has attended the progress of this measure, the men
who were opposed to it have been enabled to array
against it all the prejudices and prepossessions of
the people of the loyal slave States, and to create
the impression that the object of the measure is to
secure the abolition of slavery in those States.  This
impression has been created by the most unscrupulous
means.  The gentlemen who have opposed the
measure have not only denounced it as an attempt
to destroy the Union, but they have also represented
it as a scheme to abolish slavery in the loyal States.
The fact is, that the measure was intended to secure
the cooperation of the loyal men of all the States in
the work of restoring the Union, and not to interfere
with slavery in the States where it now exists.  But
the opponents of the measure have succeeded in
creating the impression that it is a scheme to abolish
slavery in the loyal States, and they have thus arrayed
against it all the prejudices and prepossessions of
the people of those States.  The result has been
that the measure has been delayed, and the
Administration embarrassed by the opposition which
has been brought against it.  By this delay the
