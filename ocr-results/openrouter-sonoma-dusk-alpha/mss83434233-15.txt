was Grant's second assault on Cold Harbor on June 3. After
having arrived from Hanover Town where 50,000 troops
had been massed, James Grant Wilson by June 1st
crossed the Pamunkey River and advanced toward Cold Harbor.  James H. Wilson, in the van of the advance, crossing the Totopotomoy Creek, came upon the Confederate line at 6:30 p.m. on May 30th and the fight began.  The Confederates had fortified their position along the ridge with the 6th
Corps at the left flank.[*Note: about 5,000 were
engaged.*]  This was the first fight of any size since the battle of Spottsylvania.  Grant's object, as he says in his memoirs, was to find out the enemy's strength and to move his own army across the Pamunkey.  The attack was unsuccessful, and the 9th Corps was withdrawn before the city.  The 18th Corps was held in reserve.  In the ground between himself and the Rebel line, Grant lay for five days, while the latter, opening the
levees, flooded the intervening ground, though
the attack was made in the early morning and the men were wading through water and mud to their knees.  For the most part, however, they had fired
volleys before the enemy was aroused.  Of the 7,000 men engaged, 3,500 were killed or wounded.  The Confederate loss was slight.  The colored troops did not take part in the assault.
