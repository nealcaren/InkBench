CHAPTER XVIII.

The Yorktown Sesquicentennial

The celebration of the Yorktown Sesquicentennial was held in October, 1931. This was the one hundred fiftieth anniversary of the surrender of Cornwallis at Yorktown, which virtually ended the War of the Revolution and secured the independence of our country. Among the various events of the program was a reenactment of the surrender of the British forces to the American and French armies.

The Valley Forge Express from Pittsburgh was the first train to arrive in Washington. This train, which left Pittsburgh at night, stopped first at Alexandria and then at Arlington Cemetery, where a wreath was placed on the tomb of the Unknown Soldier. Then on to the White House, where President Hoover greeted the passengers.

The Lincoln Highway Pilgrimage, starting from New York, passed through Philadelphia and Baltimore on its way to Washington. White House officials were on hand to welcome the Pennsylvania and West Virginia pilgrims. The entire caravan was met at the Capitol grounds by the Washington Committee, which had charge of the entertainment of the guests. During the two days spent in Washington, the guests were shown the points of interest. How well the Washington Committee did its work can best be told by the fact that twenty-five or thirty thousand people were in the city as guests.
