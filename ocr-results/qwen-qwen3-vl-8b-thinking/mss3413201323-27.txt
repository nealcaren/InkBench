A CHAPTER OF HISTORY  

The Register recently asked the Chicago Tribune "why did not Washington and Jefferson and Madison and Hamilton and the rest of the founders of the government meet in their day and night and give the country the needed security against invasion?" The Tribune comes back with the answer that the "founding fathers" of America never thought of anything but to "build a better country" and "free men" to get the best government and to be "a great nation"...

Mr. Irish Growing Old  

Nothing is sadder in this world than the change that comes over so many men as they shift from the buoyant enthusiasm of youth to the view-with-alarm dependency of age.  

We do not know of any illustration to the point than the speeches of John P. Irish. He was now making a speech for his new country when he felt that "the army of the United States must be under the command of a man who has been a leader of his country and has lived through its wars." This statement was made in 1881. At that time he was in his eighties.  

Mr. Irish was a forward looking man in the old days when with spontaneous eloquence he pledged himself to the idea of "a strong government," "an army" to "keep out all the dangers of the enemy," and "to make a country safe." Now he is as solemnly alarmed as any other pessimist. He says, "The army must be under the command of a man who has been a leader of his country and has lived through its wars." He says this in 1881. Now he is as solemnly alarmed as any other pessimist.  

In his earlier days, Mr. Irish believed "in the American system," as he stated, "in the principles that guide our government and in the military organization of the United States." His speeches were made in his early years. He was a member of the Illinois legislature in 1872. He was also a member of the state committee on military affairs in 1876.  

The Register says of his recent appearance on the military question: "When the war of 1812 was impending, Mr. Irish said, 'I will not fight in the militia. The militia is the best army in the world.' That is the man who now says, 'We must have a strong army, and we must not have a weak one, but we must have a strong one.'" This is a change in his attitude. Now he has changed from his former position to a position in which he is very much concerned with military affairs.  

Mr. Irish is now growing old. He has been in the United States for over 100 years. He has seen many changes. He has seen the country grow from a small nation into a great one. He has seen the country go from a weak government to a strong one.  

The Register says, "The army of the United States must be under the command of a man who has been a leader of his country and has lived through its wars." This is what Mr. Irish said in 1881. Now he is as solemnly alarmed as any other pessimist.  

When John P. Irish Favored Suffrage  

In the legislative session of 1870 an amendment proposing woman suffrage was passed by both houses of the legislature. It was enacted in the senate of 1872. The amendment was not submitted for the approval of the people by the general assembly. This amendment was far-reaching and read as follows: Article II, section 1, "The legislative power of the state shall be exercised, and shall be limited to all subjects which shall be necessary and proper for the peace, safety, and general welfare of the state. To enable the legislature to carry out their duties in the proper manner, they shall have power to tax the people for the support of the state and of the county. And they shall have power to make all such laws as may be necessary for the protection and preservation of the state."
