Turning In On Moscow Seven Germans were sentenced to prison the other day for listening to a radio tuned in on Moscow, two of them having their sentences lengthened to four years for the additional offense of discussing politics while the broadcast was going on. To most people who have ever happened to 'pick up Moscow' this will seem a high price to pay for either the instruction or the entertainment offered. But the same would be true of broadcasts from Berlin or Rome whenever these desert Mozart and Verdi for the arid wastes of propaganda. Whether Russians are being sent to jail for tuning in on the fuchner, or the duce, has not been reported, but it is hard to imagine any government being put in danger by the dreary stuff that is being broadcast in prodigious quantities for consumption abroad. It would save all the dictators much trouble simply to let the broadcasts cancel out, assuming, for example, that Russian broadcasts do no more damage in Germany than German broadcasts are doing in Russia. To admit, by police action, that this is not the case seems like a tactic of warfare. If Dr. Goebbels has not yet managed to bring the radio armament of Germany up to the Russian level of efficiency it is time for a shakeup in the ministry of propaganda.

IMPOSSIBLE COMBINATION  
To the Editor of The Republican:-  
If the subject were not so tragic  
one might be amused at the futile  
and almost frantic efforts made in  
the attempt to reduce the number of  
automobile fatalities.  
We continue to produce faster and  
more powerful cars, knowing full well  
that the greater the power and speed  
the more difficult are they to control  
in an emergency. We deplore that  
there are so many drivers whose mental  
or moral balance is not up to the  
standard required for the safe opera-  
tion of these machines, yet we provide  
operator's licenses for nearly all who  
apply.  
And as if this laxity did not allow  
enough reckless morons on the high-  
ways, we make it as easy as possible  
to procure the drug which can so im-  
pair the judgment and skill of any  
driver as to beat the automobile law  
with its deadly danger.  
In short, we demand more safety,  
more speed and more alcohol. This  
combination is impossible and every-  
one knows it. Yet we still make  
blindly, as if we are trying to beat  
the automobile law with its deadly  
danger.  
We make one of these characters ex-  
claim, "Lord, what fools these mortals  
be!" FRED W. PACKER.
