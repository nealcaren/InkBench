WASHINGTON SLAVERY.

In Washington slavery exists in a form more horrible than in the cotton or sugar growing districts of the far south. The slave there is liable to be sold at any moment, and sent away for life to a distant part of the country. He is surrounded by temptations to theft, and other crimes, to escape the cruel hand of the task-master. He is in the midst of a population who regard him with contempt, and who are ready to inflict upon him the severest punishments for the slightest offence. The following extract from a speech delivered in Congress, by John Quincy Adams, will show the opinion of that distinguished statesman on this subject. "Sir, I call the attention of the House to the fact, that in this District slavery exists in a form more horrible than in any other part of the country. Here, in the capital of the nation, where liberty is proclaimed to the world, the chains of the slave are forged anew every day."

AUCTION SALE OF SLAVES.

A few days since, we witnessed the sale of a number of slaves at public auction, in this city. The scene was truly heart-rending. Husbands were separated from their wives, parents from their children, without regard to their agonized entreaties. One woman, on seeing her little boy put up for sale, rushed forward, and clasping him in her arms, exclaimed, "O, master, don't sell my child! let me go with him!" But the auctioneer, with brutal indifference, tore the child from her embrace, and knocked him down to the highest bidder. Such are the scenes daily transpiring in the District of Columbia.

LAWS RELATIVE TO SLAVES.

The laws of the District in relation to slaves are of the most sanguinary character. By one of them, a slave is deprived of the right of trial by jury, and may be punished by his master, or by order of a justice of the peace, with any number of lashes not exceeding twenty. Another law forbids the emancipation of slaves, unless security be given that the freed person will not become a charge upon the county. These laws are a disgrace to a free government, and call loudly for reform.

TESTIMONY OF A SLAVE.

The following testimony was given by a slave who escaped from Washington: "I was born and brought up in the city of Washington. My master was a member of Congress. I was often whipped for the most trifling offences. Once, for breaking a glass, I received fifty lashes. The slave market was near our house, and I have seen hundreds of my fellow creatures sold like cattle. I determined to seek my freedom, and one dark night I fled to the north."

The above facts are sufficient to show the horrible nature of slavery in the capital of our country. Let the people awake to a sense of their duty, and demand the immediate abolition of this crying evil.

IMPRISONMENT OF SLAVES.

In the jail of Washington, there is a department set apart for the confinement of slaves. Here they are incarcerated for safe keeping, or to compel them to work out fines imposed for offences. The treatment they receive is severe in the extreme. They are fed on the coarsest food, and are often kept in solitary confinement for weeks. The following is an extract from the report of the warden: "The slaves under my charge are treated with all the humanity compatible with the safety of the institution. They are allowed a sufficiency of plain food, and are permitted to labor in the open air when the weather is favorable." But the testimony of the slaves themselves tells a different tale. They say that they are half starved, and are worked beyond their strength.
