When John P. Irish Favored Suffrage

When John P. Irish, now of Los Angeles, Cal., was a member of the Iowa house of representatives in 1870, he introduced a resolution favoring woman suffrage. The resolution was tabled. The following excerpts from the legislative record of that year tell the story:

House, Jan. 24, 1870.

Mr. Irish offered the following:

Whereas, The right of suffrage is a natural and inalienable right, and should be exercised by all citizens alike, without distinction of sex; therefore,

Resolved, That the right of suffrage should be extended to the women of Iowa.

Mr. Irish moved that the resolution be made the special order for Thursday next at 11 o'clock a.m.

Lost. Yeas 35.

Mr. Irish moved to refer the resolution to the judiciary committee.

Lost. Yeas 28; nays 45.

The resolution was then laid on the table.

When John P. Irish Favored Suffrage

In the senate, Jan. 25, 1870, Senator George G. Wright offered a resolution providing for the appointment of a joint committee to consider the propriety of submitting to the people an amendment to the constitution giving women the right to vote.

The resolution was adopted.

In the house, Jan. 28, 1870, Mr. Irish offered a resolution requesting the joint committee to report at an early day.

The resolution was adopted.

The joint committee reported adversely, and the report was adopted by the house, Feb. 12, 1870, by a vote of 54 to 33.

In the senate the report was tabled by a vote of 27 to 22.

The Des Moines Leader of Feb. 14, 1870, said:

"The vote in the house on the woman suffrage question was a surprise. It was thought that the committee report would be adopted without division. The vote shows a healthy sentiment in favor of the measure. The vote in the senate to table the report was not unexpected."

John P. Irish was born in the state of Ohio in 1845. He came to Iowa in 1867 and settled in Winterset. He was elected to the legislature in 1869, and served one term. In 1877 he was appointed United States consul to Hawaii by President Hayes. He resigned in 1882 and returned to Iowa. In 1893 he went to California, where he has since resided.

Mr. Irish has always been a staunch republican, but in 1896 he supported the gold democrats. He is a brilliant writer and speaker, and is recognized as one of the foremost men of the west.

The following letters from Munich, Germany, written by an American traveler, give an interesting glimpse of conditions in that city:

Munich, April 10, 1918.

Dear Friend:

I have just returned from a visit to the American hospital here. It is a fine institution, and the work being done is splendid. The doctors and nurses are doing all they can to alleviate the suffering of the wounded soldiers.

The food situation is serious. Bread cards are issued, and one can only buy a limited amount. Meat is scarce, and prices are high.

The people are cheerful and hopeful, and believe that peace will come soon.

Your friend,

[Name illegible]

Munich, April 15, 1918.

Dear ----:

The theaters are open, and the opera house is crowded every night. The people need diversion, and the government encourages amusements.

I attended a concert last evening. The music was fine, and the audience enthusiastic.

The streets are clean, and the shops well stocked with goods, though prices are exorbitant.

Sincerely,
