Alice Stone Blackwell

Miss Blackwell

daughter of Lucy Stone, who was a leader in the woman suffrage movement in the early days. Her mother was one of the first women to speak in public on the subject of woman's rights, and she devoted her life to the cause. Alice Stone Blackwell was born in West Brookfield, Massachusetts, February 14, 1857. She was educated at the Boston University, and from 1881 to 1887 she was associate editor of the Woman's Journal, the official organ of the National American Woman Suffrage Association. In 1887 she became editor-in-chief and held that position until 1917. Under her management the paper grew in circulation and influence.

She was a member of the board of directors of the National American Woman Suffrage Association, and later of the National Woman's Party. She was also president of the Boston Equal Suffrage Association for Good Government from 1916 to 1918. Miss Blackwell has written several books, including “Lucy Stone: Pioneer of Woman's Rights” (published in 1930), and “The Woman Citizen's Library” (1914). She has translated from several languages, including Armenian and Russian. She is a pacifist and has been active in the peace movement. During the World War she was chairman of the Woman's Peace Party in Massachusetts.

Miss Blackwell has traveled extensively in Europe and has lectured on woman suffrage and peace. She lives in Boston.
