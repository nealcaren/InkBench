Examinations were given colored applicants to the medical department of the army
with a view to their appointment as hospital attendants in the service of the
Freedmen's Bureau.  The examinations were held in pursuance of orders from the
Surgeon General, and were conducted by Medical Boards composed of army medical
officers.  They were designed to test the applicants' fitness for the duties
required of them, and to exclude those who were physically or mentally
unqualified.  The examinations were held in Washington, and the applicants
were required to appear before the board at stated times.

The first examination was held on July 18, 1863, and resulted in the
appointment of seven colored men as hospital attendants.  The second
examination was held on July 25, 1863, and resulted in the appointment
of five more.  A third examination was held on August 1, 1863, and
resulted in the appointment of three more.  The examinations were
conducted with great care, and the applicants were subjected to a
thorough physical and mental examination.

The colored hospital attendants have proved themselves efficient and
faithful in the discharge of their duties, and have given entire
satisfaction to their superior officers.  They have been employed in
the hospitals of the Freedmen's Bureau, and have performed their
duties with credit to themselves and to the service.

The examinations were discontinued after August 1, 1863, in consequence
of the reduction of the number of patients in the hospitals, and the
consequent diminution of the necessity for hospital attendants.  The
colored men who were appointed have been retained in the service, and
are still performing their duties.

The examinations were of great value in securing competent and reliable
hospital attendants for the Freedmen's Bureau, and in excluding
unqualified persons from the service.  They have also served to
stimulate the colored men to prepare themselves for positions of
responsibility and trust.

The foregoing report is respectfully submitted.
