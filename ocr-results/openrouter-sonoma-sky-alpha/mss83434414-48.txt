I. The following, as briefly as possible, is a
statement of the religious condition of the people of
Liberia, from the date of the establishment of the
Colony, in 1822, to the present time, 1843.

War Dept., June 30, 1843.

For the first ten years of the Colony, the religious
instruction was principally under the charge of the
Agents of the Colonization Society.  The Rev. L. P.
Jackson, the first Colonial Agent, was a clergyman of
the Baptist denomination, and gave regular religious
instruction to the people.  The Rev. S. G. Hercules,
who succeeded him, was also a clergyman of the same
denomination, and continued the same course.  The
Rev. J. B. Pinney, the next Agent, was a Presbyterian
clergyman, and established a church of that denomina-
tion, which he continued to serve as pastor until 1833.
During his administration, the Rev. J. W. Lugenbeel,
also a Presbyterian clergyman, was appointed a mis-
sionary to the Colony, and assisted Mr. Pinney in his
pastoral labors.  In 1833, the Rev. J. B. Pinney resigned
his Agency, and the Rev. J. W. Lugenbeel succeeded
him.  Mr. Lugenbeel continued to be the pastor of the
Presbyterian church until 1838, when the Rev. A. D.
Sayre arrived as a missionary, and took charge of the
pastoral duties.  In 1841, Mr. Sayre returned to the
United States, and the Rev. B. R. Wilson, who had
arrived in 1840 as a missionary, became the pastor.
The Rev. J. W. Lugenbeel still resides in the Colony,
and occasionally preaches.

The Methodist Episcopal Church has also a church
in the Colony, which was established in 1833, by the
Rev. M. P. Smith, a colored clergyman, who was sent
out by the Missionary Society of the Methodist Episco-
pal Church.  Mr. Smith continued to be the pastor
until his death in 1837.  He was succeeded by the Rev.
J. W. Roberts, also a colored clergyman, who arrived
in 1837, and continued to be the pastor until 1840,
when he returned to the United States.  The Rev. L.
S. Leak, another colored clergyman, arrived in 1841,
and became the pastor.  He still continues to discharge
the duties of pastor.

The Baptist Church was established in the Colony
in 1836, by the Rev. H. S. White, a colored clergyman,
who was sent out by the Baptist General Convention.
Mr. White continued to be the pastor until his death
in 1840.  He was succeeded by the Rev. L. R. Johnson,
also a colored clergyman, who arrived in 1841, and still
continues to be the pastor.

Of the 1400 souls now in Liberia, about one half
are members of churches.  There are three churches,
one Presbyterian, one Methodist, and one Baptist,
with about 350 members in all.  The Presbyterian
church has about 150 members, the Methodist about
100, and the Baptist about 100.  There are also about
200 children in the schools, who receive religious in-
struction.  The whole number of persons receiving
religious instruction, either as members of churches or
as scholars in the schools, is about 550, being about
two-fifths of the whole population.

II. Of the 1400 souls now in Liberia, about one half
are members of churches.  There are three churches,
one Presbyterian, one Methodist, and one Baptist,
with about 350 members in all.  The Presbyterian
church has about 150 members, the Methodist about
100, and the Baptist about 100.  There are also about
200 children in the schools, who receive religious in-
struction.  The whole number of persons receiving
religious instruction, either as members of churches or
as scholars in the schools, is about 550, being about
two-fifths of the whole population.

The religious instruction is entirely supported by
the contributions of the people themselves, except the
salaries of the missionaries, which are paid by the
several Missionary Societies.  The people contribute
liberally for the support of their ministers and for
religious purposes.  They have built three churches,
one for each denomination, at a cost of about $3000.

The moral and religious condition of the people is
good.  They are generally moral and religious, and
observe the Sabbath with great strictness.  There is
no intemperance, and very little immorality of any
kind.

Approved:  Major Genl. Commn.  Schools, Religion, &c.
