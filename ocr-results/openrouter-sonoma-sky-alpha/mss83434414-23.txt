But the Commissioner has referred the matter to me, and I am directed by the Secretary of War to express his views upon the subject, and to make such suggestions as occur to me.

With regard to the old plan of the Freedmen's Bureau, it has been found by experience to be so defective that the inferior officers of the Bureau at the best will be but temporary, and will not last beyond the present.

The suggestions of this report, at the best offered with great reluctance, and with the hope that they will be acted upon with great moderation and forbearance.

In order to secure the ends which the Bureau was designed to accomplish, it is indispensable that there should be a thorough organization of its officers and management, and that the work should be carried on in a systematic and economical manner.

The end sought to be attained by the Bureau is the protection and assistance of the freedmen in their transition from slavery to freedom, and their ultimate establishment in the enjoyment of all the rights and privileges of freemen.

To this end, it is necessary that the Bureau should be placed under the control of officers who are in sympathy with its objects, and who will administer its affairs with justice and impartiality.

The first step towards the accomplishment of this object is the selection of proper persons to fill the offices of the Bureau. In making this selection, regard should be had not only to the qualifications of the applicants for office, but also to their moral character and general fitness for the position.

The second step is the assignment of these officers to their respective fields of labor. This should be done with reference to the peculiar condition of the people among whom they are to labor, and the nature of the work to be performed.

The third step is the instruction of these officers in the performance of their duties. This should be done by means of a thorough course of training, in which they shall be made acquainted with the laws and regulations of the Bureau, and the principles upon which its operations are to be conducted.

The fourth step is the supervision of these officers in the discharge of their duties. This should be done by means of frequent inspections and reports, by which the efficiency of the service may be maintained, and abuses prevented.

The fifth step is the provision for the support of the Bureau. This should be done by means of a sufficient appropriation from the Treasury, and by the collection of such fees and taxes as may be necessary to meet the current expenses of the Bureau.

In conclusion, I would recommend that the Bureau be placed under the control of the War Department, and that its operations be conducted in strict conformity with the laws and regulations of the Department.

O. O. Howard,
