PRESSURE ON NEGRO PRESS IN SOUTH INCREASING

By William H. Stringer
Associated Press Staff Writer

NEW YORK (AP)—Pressure on Negro newspapers in the South to mute their criticism of segregation is increasing, a survey by the Associated Negro Press disclosed Saturday.

The survey, covering 15 leading Negro dailies and weeklies in 10 Southern states, showed that:

1. Editors are being called in for "friendly" talks with local authorities.

2. Circulation drives by white groups are being used as levers.

3. Economic reprisals are threatened against Negro businessmen who advertise.

4. In some cases, outright censorship is attempted.

The survey was made by the Associated Negro Press, a news service with headquarters here, in response to complaints from member papers.

"Editors are under constant pressure to play down stories of racial tension," said Frank L. Stanley, president of the ANP. "Some are yielding; others are fighting back."

One editor in Alabama reported he was visited by the local sheriff after publishing an account of a lynching. "He suggested I might find it healthier to tone down my reporting," the editor said.

In Mississippi, a weekly publisher said white merchants threatened to withdraw advertising if he continued to criticize segregation laws.

Circulation pressure was reported in Georgia, where a white civic group offered to buy up all available copies of a Negro paper to keep it from readers.

The survey listed these examples:

—In Texas, an editor was warned by a grand jury foreman that "trouble" might follow publication of certain stories.

—In Louisiana, a daily was accused of "stirring up unrest" after reporting on school integration efforts.

—In South Carolina, economic boycotts were threatened against advertisers in a Negro weekly.

Despite the pressures, most editors said they intend to continue objective reporting. "We can't let fear dictate our policy," one said.

The ANP survey emphasized that the pressures are not uniform but are increasing in intensity as the South faces growing civil rights activity.

NEGRO PUBLISHERS HEAR PLEA FOR CALM IN FIGHT AGAINST BIAS

ATLANTA (AP)—Speakers at the annual convention of the National Newspaper Publishers Association appealed Friday for calm but firm action in the fight against racial bias.

The association, representing 40 Negro newspapers, heard Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. urge non-violent resistance as the most effective weapon.

"We must meet hate with love, violence with non-violence," King said.

Other speakers included Roy Wilkins of the NAACP and A. Philip Randolph of the Brotherhood of Sleeping Car Porters.

The convention, attended by 200 publishers and editors, discussed ways to combat economic pressures on Negro papers in the South.
