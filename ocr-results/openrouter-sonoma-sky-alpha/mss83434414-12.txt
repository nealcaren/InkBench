VI. Report of Freedmen's Bureau:

Freedmen's Bureau

Hon. E. M. Stanton, Secretary of War.

Sir: I have the honor to submit the following report of the operations of this Bureau, from its organization to the present time. The report is made in compliance with your order of the ---, and is intended to embrace a general view of the operations of the Bureau in all its branches.

The President of the United States, in his message to Congress of Dec. 8, 1863, referred to the condition of the freedmen, and recommended that provision be made for their education and support. In pursuance of this recommendation, Congress, on the 3d of March, 1865, passed an act establishing a Bureau in the War Department, for the relief of freedmen and refugees.

This action opened the way for the organization of the Bureau, which was effected under the direction of Maj. Gen. O. O. Howard, Commissioner. The act provides that the Bureau shall be under the immediate control of the Secretary of War, and that its operations shall be conducted by the Commissioner, under such regulations as he may prescribe, subject to the approval of the Secretary of War.

The duties of the Bureau, as defined by the act, are to provide for the freedmen and refugees: 1st. By furnishing them with food, clothing, and fuel, when necessary. 2d. By assisting them in obtaining employment. 3d. By protecting them in their rights. 4th. By providing for their education. 5th. By regulating their contracts for labor. 6th. By establishing and maintaining schools. 7th. By superintending the administration of justice in cases where the parties are of different races.

The Bureau has been organized in all the States where its operations are necessary, and is now in full operation. The number of officers and agents employed is about ---, and the number of freedmen and refugees under its care is estimated at ---.

The operations of the Bureau have been divided into several distinct branches, each under the charge of a separate officer. The principal branches are: 1. The subsistence branch. 2. The labor branch. 3. The education branch. 4. The land branch. 5. The justice branch.

I. The Subsistence Branch. This branch has furnished rations to a large number of freedmen and refugees, who were unable, from age, infirmity, or other causes, to support themselves. The number of rations issued during the year ending June 30, 1866, was ---. The cost of these rations was --- dollars.

II. The Labor Branch. This branch has been engaged in assisting the freedmen in obtaining employment, and in regulating their contracts for labor. A large number of contracts have been made, by which the freedmen have been employed on plantations, and in various mechanical pursuits. The wages paid have varied from --- to --- dollars per month, with provisions.

III. The Education Branch. This branch has been very active in establishing and maintaining schools for the freedmen. There are now in operation about --- schools, with an average attendance of --- scholars. The schools are supported by contributions from the American Missionary Association, the New England Freedmen's Aid Society, and other benevolent associations, and by appropriations from the funds of the Bureau.

IV. The Land Branch. This branch has been charged with the duty of assigning to freedmen abandoned and confiscated lands, under the provisions of the act of Congress. A considerable quantity of land has been assigned, and is now occupied by freedmen, who are cultivating it with success.

V. The Justice Branch. This branch has been engaged in adjusting differences between freedmen and their employers, and in protecting the freedmen in their rights. A large number of cases have been disposed of, and the results have been generally satisfactory.

The operations of the Bureau have been attended with many difficulties, arising from the prejudices of the people, the hostility of the rebel element, and the want of funds. But notwithstanding these obstacles, much good has been accomplished, and the condition of the freedmen has been greatly improved.

Very respectfully, your obedient servant,

O. O. Howard,  
Major-General, Commissioner.

Bureau Refugees, Freedmen, and Abandoned Lands,  
Washington, D.C., July 1, 1866.
