Preface

We most deeply deplore the institution of slavery, the source of so many evils to master and slave; and we wish for its abolition so far as it can be accomplished with justice and safety to the whole country. But we cannot, on this account, consent to the destruction of the Union to which we owe our existence, and under whose auspices we have so long prospered. And we have the fullest confidence that the people of the South will not, by any unjust legislation, deprive the people of the North of their Constitutional rights, or do any act to destroy the Federal Union. On the contrary, we believe that they will cheerfully unite with us in every proper and constitutional effort to secure to the slave the enjoyment of all his natural rights, and to mitigate as much as possible the evils of his condition.

The condition of slavery is so repugnant to the feelings of our people, that we should hail its abolition as a blessing, whenever it can be accomplished without endangering the peace and safety of the country. But if the abolition of slavery be the price of the preservation of the Union, we are not prepared to pay it. We had rather see the Union dissolved than slavery abolished by force.
