  Sir, I believe this war was inevitable. This insane ambition and mad, craving lust of the South could be checked alone by the results of war. It had closed its ears hrmetically against the voice of persuasion and reason. And wherever slavery existed that ambition and that lust had root. Slavery did cause this war. It was destined cause war and if not put in process of eradication, will involve our posterity war. It is not fitting, therefore, that the result of war shall be the end of slavery ? The President's proclamation does not pro- pose to touch the institution in the Border States. But, as I have said, with the market for the annual crop gone, it will be found to be of no more value in Kentucky than it is now found to be in Missouri, with her free surroundings. And then we will come to what I am prepared to say very few words upon, the compensated emancipation proposal of the President. In this rescinded millions, the millions of millions that we have

  Missouri is here asking $10,000,000 on condition that she emancipates here slaves within little more than half a year. In God's name, let us give it to her; and if Kentucky and Mary- land make the same claim, let us give it to them, and pay our full share out of the results of our own hard labor at the North. Let us even, by an addition of our already grievous burden of taxes imposed by this war--slavery's own greengrocer--share the losses of those whose slaves shall be exiled into freemen. But, say the gentlemen, the proclamation is unconstitutional and illegal, and therefore void. I fear self-interest blinds some of them.
