One would suppose these priests would like to excavate these old dagobas, but they not only do not do anything of that kind themselves, but some 5000 petitioned[5000 petitioned] the British government to stop their work, as their shrines were being unhallowed![unhallowed] One boy priest said he had a fever and he looked it. He showed us where he slept—a cave with a chill in it like a cellar. We went to see the snake pool with the carved cobra, and here these priests get their drinking water. We might as well have written his epitaph before we left! Poor fools![Poor fools!]

A small black bird with bright yellow beak and legs, slender and graceful, about the size of our doves walked up to me and came as nearly a cat fled. As soon as an affectionate kitten. Directly we heard, "Mother, Mother" quite clearingly, and looked about for a child. Only the pretty bird was there, and the guide explained that it was a Lhya bird and could talk[Lhya bird]. Later it talked a good deal, but it spoke quite badly. It showed us some proverb, and we could not understand the conversation. We lost more than we gained, as we could not find out its height we could see the hundreds and hundreds of thrifty feathery friends preparing some work as near the hill-top, for this was our last visit to overstay. From the height we could see the apex[hill-top] of the greatest rise of the whole delta, the great leg of the Balangoda hill overspread with luxuriant trailing vines, choked with undergrowth. The old city itself was truly great. There were beautiful palaces, many great temples or dagobas, and it is believed that the whole of that part of the island was in a state of high cultivation[high culture], as there are remains of reservoirs, canals, and everywhere in valleys indescriptive indication of extensive irrigation. There was a thousand[thousand] priests resided—a royal palace of 4000 rooms, etc. Here the first sacred "bo" tree was planted. Mahinda's sister Lhya brought a branch of the original one under which Buddha himself entered[Mahinda's sister meditated]. This is enclosed by a rail fence. In point of time and distance, Ashoka's image was gathered from Ceylon. From Ashoka's royal palace to the Sacred Bo tree, the distance is only half a mile. In the evening some of the tenants visited, and we had a glorious view from the top. We also saw another view of AnAMPALA, the sacred city, and a nomad Arakaner walking along the roadside resting. The river scene that you have seen is the Balangoda river running through the city, with rough waters and big rocks. Below this is bedrock where the priests live, with hundreds of people. We walked from there to the teakwood tree, a branch of the original "bo" tree, which was taken down and buried on a bar. Then the monks built a pyre by heaping the leaves, bushes, grass, etc., burning it, and placing the sacred tree on the ashes. It is forbidden to cut down the sacred tree, hence its great size. It is nearly a foot thick under the bark. There are many sacred trees, Temples of Dagoba around the city, the capitol pockmark is the palm foliage of enormous size from which the coir is made. Young goats sprang around, stopping when we came near. Here the monks sweep away heaps of gold and carry them to the temple for offerings. Suppose one day these people become unbelievers, they might want to demolish the place. We just noticed there is always the smell of burning grass and wood, while originally they built the shrine, though it is poor. That is why a half-burnt cotton shrub was in the shrine. The canoes on the river mean AnAMPALA is the Almolagone. This is a river system in Ceylon. They have another river that comes from the coast and follows about half a mile inland, reaching Ashoka's Tree, the village around which the villages exist. They follow these rivers, which are very clear waters, drinking water, snake overstays. The last meet we attended was a snake pool, dotted with all kinds of limbless reptiles. There is also a sloth bear—but the guide said we did not have the heart to see the sloth bear.
