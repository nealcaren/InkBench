

26 FREEDMEN’S BUREAU SOUTH CAROLINA AND GEORGIA.

General Rufus Saxton, as you are aware, had charge of matters pertaining to freemen in South Carolina during most of the war, having been enabled by the operations of the army during the winter of 1864–65 to visit Beaurevoir and St. Helena islands. In these islands, he established settlements, labor regulations, and thriving schools under his direction, which cared for a thousand negro refugees captured by our armies. These refugees, previously in a state of destruction and disarray, were integrated into the system of schools and labor camps.

The Assistant Commissioner in charge, Col O. Osborne, faced his own challenges while on duty, reporting injuries, a serious wound sustained during an engagement, and difficulties with the transportation of a cargo of sick soldiers to Wilmington, Delaware. Throughout this period, General Saxton oversaw the extension of the Bureau’s operations into South Carolina and Georgia, setting up headquarters in various locations, including Beaufort, Hilton, and Charleston.

Reports from South Carolina and Georgia, at the end of September, highlighted issues such as tardiness among teachers, violent confrontations, fraudulent labor practices, military withdrawals, and illegal operations on the islands. Despite these challenges, Saxton managed over a thousand contracts, with some planters experiencing labor struggles. Additionally, the growth in schools was noted, with 114 schools, 174 classrooms, and 9,500 scholars.

Colonel Saxton also dealt with internal conflicts among officers and Assistant Commissioners, such as trooping-on Whiting and disruptions by General Saxton’s employees in Hilton Head. He noted the presence of colored regiments and the apprehensions among whites regarding the colored troops joining freedmen. Meanwhile, Col. A. C. Kelsey, who had been at Beaufort, was placed under Saxton’s command.

FREEMEN’S BUREAU MICHIGAN.

In a discourse to the necessity of the Bureau’s presence in the South, the Commissioner of the Freedmen’s Bureau, Bryant B. Moore, addressed the assembly, underscoring the challenges faced by newly freed individuals. He recounted stories of how colored people eagerly sought education, as seen through young black boys at libraries showing eagerness to learn.

He elaborated on the efforts to establish schools in South Carolina, including the significant investments by various agents, and how the Bureau’s role was to provide assistance and guidance rather than direct aid. He addressed charges against Bureau agents, affirming their integrity and detailing the failures of some unscrupulous contract signers who withheld pay from laborers.

The Commissioner spoke about the need for armed protection for Union forces, the role of former soldiers in protecting refugees, and outlined plans to set up a color-blind justice system. He mentioned various agents like Col. Manly, conducted explorations for settlements, and the unresolved status of land claims in South Carolina and Georgia due to conflicting landowners. He also stressed the importance of cooperation across political differences for the welfare of freedmen.

The Commissioner urged a collective effort for the advancement of freedmen, emphasizing vigilance against prejudice and the need for educated teachers, demanding the attention of legal authorities. He criticized violence, citing acts against agents in South Carolina and attributed some conflicts to generals not enforcing orders effectively. He also highlighted the critical matter of William Lloyd Garrison’s conflict with Bureau officials and plans to protect the educational efforts of the South.

This fortnight’s report included myriad specific mentions: Agent Cooper and Agent Jackson, the layout of school districts in Beaufort, numbers of recruits and draft registrations, the distribution of subsistence tickets, and the names of various agents like Jackson, Walker, and Wilson. It further lists conflicts, including injuries, deaths, and incidents of violence, reflecting the turbulent conditions of the South during reconstruction efforts.
