

**"Natchez National Cemetery, Mississippi"** is a historical and genealogical record documenting the Union soldiers interred in the Natchez National Cemetery, located in Natchez, Mississippi. Published in 1910, the book serves as a meticulously compiled register of Union soldiers buried there, along with some records of Confederate soldiers, reflecting post-Civil War reinterment practices.

### Key Features:
1. **Purpose**:  
   - The book was created to catalog Union soldiers buried in the cemetery, which was established after the Civil War. Some Confederate soldiers are also listed, indicating their reburial or relocation in line with reconciliation efforts.

2. **Structure**:  
   - The records are organized alphabetically by name, with each entry listing:
     - Rank
     - Regiment
     - Date of death (some entries are blank, indicating uncertain records)
     - Grave number
     - Original place of burial (location in Mississippi or elsewhere)
   - Officers from Confederate states are included in a separate section, noted with their respective ranks and regiments.

3. **Historical Context**:  
   - The cemetery houses soldiers from the American Civil War era, with entries spanning battles and campaigns such as Baton Rouge, Vicksburg, and Mobile Bay. Some entries extend to the 19th century, showcasing the cemetery's role in honoring veterans from multiple conflicts.

4. **Significance**:  
   - A valuable resource for genealogists, historians, and descendants of Civil War soldiers, as it preserves details of individuals who served in the Union Army and were laid to rest in this national cemetery.
   - The inclusion of Confederate soldiers highlights post-war reconciliation efforts, where some Confederate remains were relocated to national cemeteries.

5. **Content Examples**:  
   - Union soldiers listed include Corporal John Atkinson (1st Illinois Infantry, buried in Tombigbee, Mississippi) and Sergeant Henry Allen (58th U.S. Colored Cavalry, buried in 1864).  
   - Confederate officers like Captain F. Jones (5th Alabama Cavalry) and Captain J.L. Sims (58th Alabama Volunteer Infantry) are noted, reflecting their reburial in the cemetery.

### Publication Details:  
- **Author/Publisher**: Likely compiled by government officials or volunteers associated with the U.S. Army's cemetery management, though no specific author is credited.  
- **Historic Context**: Published in 1910, during a period of increased interest in Civil War memorialization and veteran records.

### Notes:  
- The title may be slightly misleading, as the cemetery includes Confederate soldiers. This reflects post-war efforts to consolidate burials and promote national unity.  
- Missing entries in some columns suggest incomplete records or reliance on post-war documentation.

This book remains a critical resource for understanding post-Civil War burial practices and the history of James National Cemetery—one of the original 70 national cemeteries established for Union soldiers.
