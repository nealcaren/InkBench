

Gordon Parks was born in Fort Scott, Kansas on November 30, 1912. When his mother died, he had to leave home at fifteen. For the next twelve years, he lived mainly in Minneapolis, Minnesota, working as a piano player, bus boy, dining car waiter, Civilian Conservation Corpsman, and professional basketball player before taking up photography in the late 1930s and moving to Chicago.

In 1942, he was awarded the first Julius Rosenwald Fellowship in photography and chose to work with Roy Stryker at the Farm Security Administration (FSA) in Washington, D.C. During World War II, he was an OWI (Office of War Information) correspondent. Later, he joined Stryker at Standard Oil Company in New York as a photographer, working out of New York City and living in White Plains, New York. At that time, he wrote his first two books on photography and began doing free-lance fashion photography for *Vogue* and *Glamour* magazines.

In 1949, he joined the staff of *LIFE* magazine and worked as a photojournalist until 1968, completing over 300 assignments and articles on varied subjects. In the 1960s, he gained fame for his stories on Black leaders, later compiled into his book *Born Black*. Gordon Parks occasionally wrote and photographed for *LIFE* while pursuing other projects. He co-founded *Essence* magazine, serving as its editorial director from 1970 to 1973.

His film career began in 1961 with the documentary *Flavio*, the story of a boy from a Brazilian slum. Another celebrated *LIFE* story, *Diary of a Harlem Family* (1968), won an Emmy Award. His first Hollywood film was *The Learning Tree* (1969), which he produced, directed, and wrote the musical score for. He later directed *Shaft*, *Shaft’s Big Score* (for which he composed the classic musical score), *Super Cops*, and *Leadbelly*.

Gordon Parks’ achievements include writing 13 books, producing 7 motion pictures and 3 documentaries, composing 13 major musical works (classical, blues, and popular), and authoring articles, poetry, and essays published in major publications and museums. His best-selling book, *The Learning Tree* (1969), remains in print in 9 languages.

Mr. Parks received numerous awards for photography, writing, filmmaking, and humanitarianism, including the NAACP’s Spingarn Medal, Governor’s Medal of Merit, and honorary degrees in literature, fine arts, and humane letters from colleges and universities across America.

John F. Kennedy High School in New York is named after him, having honored him with an honorary school diploma shortly before his graduation during the Depression.

Mr. Parks has three children, three grandchildren, and one great-grandchild.
