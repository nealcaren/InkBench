ANCIENT ENGLISH DOCTRINE OF CONSTRUCTIVE TREASON.

Treason, as defined in the law of England, at the date of the constitution, embraced many misdemeanors which are not now held to be crimes. Offences of a political character, not accompanied with any intention to subvert the government; mere words of disrespect to the ruling sovereign; assaults upon the king’s officers or court; and many other striking one of the judges of the nature of treason, were, in ancient times, declared treason by Parliament, or so construed by judges, as to constitute that crime.
