A CHAPTER OF HISTORY.
The Register recently asked of the Chicago Tribune "why did not Washington and Madison and Jefferson and Hamilton and the constitution, which they framed, provide for the right of women to vote?"

The Register has been answered by James Madison, Jr., in the columns of the Tribune. The answer is that the constitution was framed under the reign of John Adams. The constitution was a compromise, and the fathers did not want to risk the success of their work by introducing a new element.

The constitution was adopted for the promotion of the general welfare, and the fathers were not wholly satisfied with it, but they were willing to adopt it as a temporary experiment.

WOMEN IN COLORADO.
Washington Favors Use of The Des Moines Register.
Member John P. Irish, of Colorado, formerly of Des Moines, has written a letter to the Register, in which he says that he voted for woman suffrage in Colorado in 1877, and that he has never regretted it.

When John P. Irish Favored Suffrage

In the legislative session of 1870 a bill was introduced to submit to the people a proposition to amend the state constitution so as to give women the right to vote. John P. Irish, then a member of the house, made a speech in favor of the amendment which was submitted to popular vote. John P. Irish said:

"I have, having done so much to secure the submission of the question to the people, I shall not attempt to argue both sides of the question. I shall only say that the experiment be demonstrated a long time in ignorance and disorder, which we wish should throw any discredit upon the family, or upon the experiment. For who is in a more helpless condition than a woman or a more heartfelt interest in the welfare of the race?"

This amendment was far-reaching and read as follows:

"Sec. 1. The general assembly may, at any time, by a two-thirds vote of the members elected to each house, and published for three months previous to the next general election, submit to the people the question of universal suffrage, and if a majority of the electors voting at such election shall vote for it, at the next session of the general assembly it shall be the duty of the general assembly to submit the question of universal suffrage to the people, and if a majority of the electors voting thereon shall vote for it, it shall thereafter be part of the constitution of the state."

In a second speech, Mr. Irish said:

"Mr. Chairman—I am requested by the committee to read certain remarks of Mr. Ball, which I shall do. Mr. Ball said:

'There are those who have completed their fourscore years, and to them the question of our homes and firesides is of more importance than the question of the ballot. Remember that the ballot is the weapon of the strong, and the weak must look to the strong for protection. I have no fears for the ballot in the hands of the wise and good, but I have fears for the ballot in the hands of the ignorant and vicious. I know it is said that they will learn to use it, but I do not believe that they will learn to use it in a day. I have no fears for the ballot in the hands of the wise and good, but I have fears for the ballot in the hands of the ignorant and vicious.'

"Mr. Chairman, I have no such fears. I believe that the ballot in the hands of woman will be a power for good. I believe that the ballot in the hands of woman will be a power for good, and I believe that the ballot in the hands of woman will be a power for good. I believe that the ballot in the hands of woman will be a power for good, and I believe that the ballot in the hands of woman will be a power for good."

WHAT MR. IRISH SAID.
The Register has obtained a copy of the speech made by Mr. Irish in the Iowa legislature of 1870. The speech is as follows:

"Mr. Chairman: I shall not attempt to argue both sides of the question. I shall only say that the experiment be demonstrated a long time in ignorance and disorder, which we wish should throw any discredit upon the family or upon the experiment. Perhaps they are degraded. Perhaps they are ignorant. But if so, it is because they have not had the ballot. Give them the ballot, and they will learn to use it. I have no fears for the ballot in the hands of woman. I believe that the ballot in the hands of woman will be a power for good."
